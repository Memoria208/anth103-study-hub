## The Archaeology of the Mind

## Big Picture: What This Chapter Is About

Processual archaeology in the 1960s–80s focused on things archaeologists could recover with confidence — environment, technology, subsistence — and treated the "cognitive" side of culture (religion, belief, ideas) as unimportant epiphenomena. Chapter 12 makes the case for an **archaeology of the mind**: since all humans interpret their world symbolically, and not just materially, archaeologists can't fully understand the past without also studying worldview, ritual, and symbolic behavior. The chapter's central tension is methodological: symbols have **no necessary connection** to their meanings, so how can an archaeologist ever know what an ancient symbol "meant"? The chapter works through several case studies — Dorset polar bear carvings, Stonehenge, and Upper Paleolithic cave art — to show which approaches hold up to scientific testing and which collapse into unfalsifiable guesswork.

## Part 1: Cognitive Archaeology and Symbols

**Q: What is cognitive archaeology, and why were processual archaeologists "lukewarm" toward it?**
A: Cognitive archaeology studies the aspects of ancient culture produced by the human mind — perception and classification of the universe, ideas about the supernatural, the values that govern societies, and how those ideas are expressed in art. Processual archaeologists avoided it because the archaeological record relating to "ideas" seemed too shaky and ambiguous to approach scientifically; they treated cognitive/religious aspects of culture as **epiphenomena** — derivative features unimportant to understanding the past compared to technology and economy.

**Q: What is the postprocessual argument for an "archaeology of the mind"?**
A: That heavy materialism dehumanizes the past. An archaeology of the mind emphasizes the values, ideas, and beliefs that make us human, and necessarily has a more postprocessual flavor because it recovers *meanings* rather than law-like generalizations. Even so, symbolic perspectives can be scientifically tested if researchers can build appropriate linkages between the interpretation of ancient symbols and behaviors directly inferable from the archaeological record.

**Q: What is a symbol, and how does it differ from a sign?**
A: A **symbol** is an object or act (verbal or nonverbal) that, by cultural convention, stands for something else with which it has no necessary connection (e.g., a red circle with a diagonal line). A **sign** has an actual connection to what it signifies (e.g., a cigarette icon inside that circle actually depicts a cigarette). Because symbols have no necessary connection to their meaning, an outsider cannot deduce a symbol's meaning just by looking at it — you have to be enculturated into it, or have ethnographic/historical information about the culture that made it.

**Q: Why does this make symbols so hard to study archaeologically?**
A: If there's no necessary link between a symbol and what it stands for, an archaeologist encountering a symbol with no living cultural context has no reliable way to reconstruct its meaning. This is the central methodological problem the rest of the chapter wrestles with.

**Q: What is the Food Taboos in the Near East case, and what does it teach about materialist vs. symbolic explanations?**
A: Two explanations for the ancient prohibition on eating pork:
- **Marvin Harris (materialist)**: Pigs are forest/wet-environment animals poorly suited to the desert; the taboo reflects the high cost of raising them there.
- **Mary Douglas (symbolic)**: Argued you can't explain one tabooed food (pork) without explaining the whole list (shellfish, rabbits, camels, insects, birds) in Leviticus. She found the prohibited animals were "unclean" in the sense of **violating the cultural order of creation** (e.g., pigs have cloven hooves but don't chew cud, making them an anomaly among edible animals). Douglas's approach explains the entire list, but raises the further question of what generated that Near Eastern worldview in the first place.

## Part 2: Polar Bears and Dorset History

**Q: Who were the Dorset, and what is unusual about their material culture?**
A: The **Dorset culture** were nomadic hunter-gatherers active in the North American Arctic (Dorset appeared ~2500 years ago / 500 BC, and vanished ~AD 1300, a century after the ancestors of the modern Inuit arrived ~800 years ago). They subsisted on seals, caribou, musk ox, and fish, living in small square houses. They are known for small (2–5 cm), detailed carvings of bone, antler, and walrus ivory — most commonly polar bears and human/humanlike faces, some bears bearing a vertical slit in the throat rubbed with red ochre.

**Q: Why were polar bears "special" to the Dorset, according to Betts, Hardenberg, and Stirling?**
A: The Dorset (unlike the later Inuit) apparently hunted seals without boats, from the shoreline and ice edges — exactly where polar bears also hunt. Bears look humanlike when moving on all fours or standing upright, and a bear's skeleton resembles a human skeleton more than any other animal's. The researchers argue Dorset carvings faithfully depict specific bear hunting poses (stalking, standing still, aquatic stalking) in frequencies that match actual bear behavior — evidence the carvings were more than idle whittling.

**Q: What is the "shared spiritual kinship" interpretation of the carvings?**
A: Many hunter-gatherer societies believe animals have a will/spirit like humans and that a successful hunt occurs only when the animal offers itself as a gift — requiring respectful treatment of its remains. At Dorset longhouse sites, bear skulls are covered in red ochre and ritually placed along walls/doorways, and some researchers suggest the longhouse itself (with its line of interior hearths) symbolically represents a bear skeleton. The "kill hole" slots in some bear and human-face carvings may actually mark a point of spiritual connection between humans and bears, not a butchering mark.

**Q: How might this cultural/spiritual connection to polar bears help explain Dorset history?**
A: As the climate warmed and the ancestral Inuit arrived with kayaks/umiaks that let them hunt from open water, the Inuit had access to more food and grew in population, while the Dorset — who intensified bear-carving activity late in their history, as if reasserting their traditional shore/ice-based identity — retreated and disappeared within about 200 years. The suggestion is that Dorset cultural identity (tied to bears and shore hunting) may have limited their ability to adapt, contributing to their disappearance.

**Q: How do religion and ritual connect to this case, conceptually?**
A: **Religion** is a society's mechanism for relating supernatural phenomena to the everyday world. **Ritual** is a succession of discrete behaviors performed in a particular order under particular circumstances to intercede with the supernatural. Rituals matter to archaeology because — unlike pure belief — they typically involve material culture that can survive in the archaeological record (e.g., ochre-covered bear skulls placed at doorways).

## Part 3: Stonehenge and the Neolithic World

**Q: What is Stonehenge physically, and what is a henge?**
A: Stonehenge sits on the Salisbury Plain in southwestern Britain. It consists of a ring of massive sarsen (sandstone) uprights capped by lintels (~25 tons each), surrounding five **trilithons** (two massive sarsens plus a lintel) arranged in a U-shape, with smaller bluestones (dolerite, 2–5 tons, transported ~180 miles from Wales) inside and around them. A **henge** is a Neolithic circular earthen bank with an interior ditch — unlike later Iron Age ditch-and-bank fortifications (ditch outside, to keep people out), henges seem designed to keep something *in*, and are probably more symbolic than functional.

**Q: What was built at the Stonehenge site before Stonehenge itself, and what is the Cursus?**
A: Hunter-gatherers erected large posts around 8000 bc. Between 3660–3370 bc, people built the Greater and Lesser **Cursus**, long oval ditch-and-bank enclosures (the Greater Cursus is 1.75 miles long) of unknown function, often associated with barrows (grave mounds). Stonehenge itself was built beginning about 3000–2920 bc, with major construction phases continuing (trilithons and sarsen circle 2620–2480 bc; the avenue 2480–2280 bc) until building activity dropped off around 2200 bc with the arrival of the Beaker folk, and ended by about 1680 bc.

**Q: What hypothesis did Michael Parker Pearson and Ramilisonina bring to Stonehenge, and where did it come from?**
A: In 1998, British archaeologist Michael Parker Pearson (an expert on the British Neolithic) worked with his Malagasy colleague Ramilisonina, who had researched Madagascar practices of erecting large stones and building elaborate tombs to honor the dead. When Parker Pearson noted that no one knew what Stonehenge's stones were for, Ramilisonina immediately assumed they honored the dead — because in Madagascar, stone symbolizes permanence (like a dead body), while organic material (wood) symbolizes the transient, living world.

**Q: What three things did Parker Pearson need to demonstrate to test this hypothesis?**
A: (1) That Stonehenge was a place of and for the dead; (2) that nearby Durrington Walls was a place of and for the living; (3) that the two sites were connected and of the same age.

**Q: What did excavations at Durrington Walls show?**
A: Durrington Walls was a Neolithic village (c. 2500 bc) occupied only about 45 years, home to several thousand people — but not year-round. Animal teeth (cattle and pigs killed at 9 and 15 months, i.e., midwinter and midsummer) show the site was used seasonally for feasting, timed to the midwinter/midsummer solstices that also align with Stonehenge's axis. Strontium isotope analysis showed feast animals came from far away (some from the Scottish Highlands), and the earlier Ring of Brodgar henge in the Orkney Islands suggests the henge idea may have spread south from Scotland.

**Q: How did Parker Pearson connect the two sites physically?**
A: His team failed to trace Stonehenge's avenue all the way to the Avon River — but instead discovered a new henge exactly where the avenue should have ended, with pits for about 25 bluestones in a 10-meter circle; chips left behind and a datable antler pick suggest these bluestones were removed and taken to Stonehenge. Separately, his team found a chalk-and-flint processional avenue running from a Durrington Walls woodhenge down to the river. Both sites, in other words, were linked to the Avon River — and to each other.

**Q: What evidence shows Stonehenge was "largely about the dead"?**
A: More than 60 sets of cremated remains have been found at Stonehenge (with an estimated 150–200 total once fully excavated), while almost no human remains were found at heavily-populated Durrington Walls. This supports the idea that people cremated bodies at their home villages, then transported remains to Stonehenge — likely reserved for the most elite (mostly males in the prime of life) — during the twice-yearly solstice gatherings.

**Q: Why is this case a model of good scientific method in cognitive archaeology?**
A: Parker Pearson began with a cultural analogy (from Madagascar) suggesting a Neolithic mindset, but then translated that idea into testable, material predictions (cremations at Stonehenge, seasonal occupation and no burials at Durrington Walls, a physical/temporal connection between the two sites) — and confirmed them empirically, rather than resting on the analogy alone.

## Part 4: Upper Paleolithic Cave Art — Competing Interpretations

**Q: What is the Upper Paleolithic, and what makes its art an "explosion"?**
A: The **Upper Paleolithic** (40,000–12,000 bp) in Europe features a complex stone/bone/antler technology plus wall art, portable art, and decorated tools. Evidence of artistic expression appears only in the last 90,000 years and becomes widespread only in the last 40,000 — while almost nothing from the preceding 5 million years of the human lineage counts as art. Cave paintings occur in roughly 200 French caves (more in Spain); much dates to the **Magdalenian** phase (18,000–12,000 bp, named for the La Madeleine rockshelter), though Grotte Chauvet's paintings (discovered 1994) date to 26,000–36,000 bp via AMS radiocarbon dating of the charcoal-based black paint.

**Q: What features of the art itself are especially puzzling?**
A: Human figures are rare and poorly executed compared to animal figures; there are no true "scenes" or narratives despite overlapping images; certain animals (horses, aurochs, bison, ibex, stags, reindeer) are emphasized far more than others (mammoths, bears, rhinos, cats); and the art is often placed in the most remote, difficult-to-reach parts of caves — sometimes requiring ropes and tolerance of dangerous carbon dioxide buildup.

**Q: What is the "art for art's sake" interpretation, and what's the problem with it?**
A: Nineteenth-century scholars viewed the art as an early expression of an innate aesthetic sense, created in newly available leisure time, with no particular meaning. David Lewis-Williams points out this is circular (an innate aesthetic sense is inferred from the art, and the art is treated as evidence of that sense) and it fails to explain why the art was placed in remote, dangerous cave locations rather than made for public display.

**Q: What is sympathetic magic, and what is the problem with that interpretation?**
A: **Sympathetic magic** rests on the principle "like controls like" — Salomon Reinach suggested images of pregnant animals promoted real fertility, and Abbé Henri Breuil suggested "killing" a painted animal ensured a successful real hunt. But bison and horses (rarely eaten, per the food-bone evidence) are the most frequently depicted animals, while red deer and reindeer (the animals actually most often eaten) are comparatively rare in the art — undercutting a straightforward hunting-magic explanation.

**Q: What is structuralism, and how did Leroi-Gourhan and Laming-Emperaire apply it to cave art?**
A: **Structuralism** holds that humans understand reality through paired oppositions (life/death, male/female) and is associated with anthropologist Claude Lévi-Strauss. Annette Laming-Emperaire pushed for systematic recording of image content, position (entrance/central/peripheral/back), and associations. André Leroi-Gourhan then used quantitative data from 66 French caves to group images into four sets (small herbivores, large herbivores, rare species, dangerous animals), associating small herbivores with "maleness" and large herbivores with "femaleness," plus "narrow" (male) and "wide" (female) abstract signs. He mapped these onto cave zones (stags/male signs at entrances and peripheries; dangerous animals at the back; both sexes' signs in central areas) and proposed the cave itself represented the "female" balancing the male imagery.

**Q: What went wrong with Leroi-Gourhan's structuralist analysis?**
A: His method involved circular reasoning (sometimes using an image to define a cave zone as "central" or "peripheral," and other times using the zone to classify an ambiguous image). His species/gender associations didn't hold up as more caves were studied. He was also influenced by the Freudian psychology popular in his own era, interpreting "narrow" and "wide" abstract symbols as male and female genitalia — a culturally-loaded interpretive leap the book flags as a caution about imposing the researcher's own cultural assumptions onto ancient symbols. Because bison/horse-gender associations in the actual images were inconsistent (sometimes reversed, sometimes ambiguous), it's also possible the animals functioned as **totems** — symbols of different clans — rather than universal male/female markers.

**Q: What is Lewis-Williams's shamanism hypothesis, and what makes it different from earlier approaches?**
A: **Shamans** are individuals who claim to access supernatural powers/spirits through trance (via drugs, fasting, dehydration, or sensory deprivation), interpreting trance visions as communication with the supernatural — a practice found in nearly all hunting-and-gathering societies (e.g., Lakota vision quests, Ju/'hoansi trance dancing). Lewis-Williams argues Upper Paleolithic cave art records what shamans saw in trance. Unlike earlier symbol-meaning approaches, this hypothesis doesn't require interpreting what a specific image symbolized — it rests on cross-cultural neurology.

**Q: What are the three neurologically-based stages of trance Lewis-Williams describes?**
A: (1) **Entoptic phenomena** — dots, grids, zigzags, nested curves, produced by the optical nervous system itself and therefore universal to all humans (uniformitarian argument); (2) the mind converts entoptic images into culturally meaningful forms (e.g., an Upper Paleolithic shaman "sees" aurochs and reindeer, a Lakota "sees" horses and tepees); (3) a sensation of moving through a tunnel/vortex, merging entoptic and representational imagery, sometimes described across cultures as "dying" or reaching the "other side."

## Part 5: The Cave of Lascaux

**Q: How was Lascaux discovered, and why is it significant?**
A: Discovered by accident in September 1940 when a dog named Robot fell into a hole, leading four French schoolboys (Marcel Ravidat, Simon Coencas, Georges Agniel, and Jacques Marsal) to find the painted cave. Abbé Henri Breuil ("Pope of Prehistory") was consulted, and the boys guarded the cave through the winter of 1940–41. Lascaux's paintings date to about 17,000 years ago; it was open to the public from 1948 until rising humidity and fungus growth forced its closure in 1963. The precise replica Lascaux II opened nearby in 1983.

**Q: How does Lewis-Williams read the layout of Lascaux in terms of trance stages?**
A: The **Hall of the Bulls** (entrance chamber) contains large, "composed" animal images along with early-stage entoptic-style abstract signs (dots, wavy lines). Moving into the narrower **Axial Gallery**, imagery becomes more merged with abstraction — including the famous upside-down "falling" horse, which Lewis-Williams interprets not as literally falling but as a representation of the trance vortex. Farther in — the **Apse** and **Diverticule of the Felines** — images become confused and overlapping, which Lewis-Williams links to the deepest stage of trance (or to one shaman symbolically layering an image over another's to draw on their power). Rare human-animal hybrid figures (walking upright, staring at the viewer) may record moments where the boundary between observer and animal collapsed in deep trance.

**Q: What role do the cave's physical contours and hidden offerings play in this interpretation?**
A: Artists used the natural bulges and contours of cave walls to bring images to life (e.g., the two charging bulls that seem to run toward a viewer in a narrow Lascaux passage). Lewis-Williams argues the cave wall was treated as a permeable membrane between the physical and spirit world, so working an image into the wall's own contours increased its symbolic power. Flint blades and bear teeth covered in ochre, jammed into cracks near some images, may represent similar attempts to reach through that membrane into the spirit world.

**Q: What is the significance of the bison and "falling man" scene in the Shaft/Well at Lascaux?**
A: At the bottom of a dangerous 5-meter pit is an image of a bison, apparently speared, with a stick-figure man (four fingers, bird-like head) falling backward in front of it, and a bird-topped pole beneath him. Its meaning remains disputed; it's unclear whether the elements were painted as a single "scene" or added at different times.

**Q: What is Lewis-Williams's overall conclusion about the meaning of Upper Paleolithic art?**
A: It is not simple decoration, and not fertility or hunting magic. It reflects an effort by Upper Paleolithic people to grapple with the reality that everyday, waking existence is not the only kind of experience there is — an attempt to understand altered states of consciousness (dreams, trance) and connect with a perceived spirit world.

## Exam-Style Practice Questions

1. Why did processual archaeologists tend to treat religion and belief as "epiphenomena," and what is the postprocessual counter-argument for an archaeology of the mind?

2. Explain the difference between a symbol and a sign. Why does that distinction make ancient symbols so difficult to interpret archaeologically?

3. Compare Marvin Harris's and Mary Douglas's explanations for the Near Eastern pork taboo. What question does Douglas's explanation leave unanswered?

4. What evidence do Betts, Hardenberg, and Stirling use to argue that Dorset ivory carvings are more than idle decoration?

5. Describe the three things Michael Parker Pearson needed to demonstrate to test the hypothesis that Stonehenge honored the dead, and summarize the evidence he found for each.

6. What is sympathetic magic, and what evidence from Upper Paleolithic food remains challenges this interpretation of cave art?

7. Explain Leroi-Gourhan's structuralist interpretation of cave art gender symbolism, and describe at least two methodological problems with his approach.

8. Summarize David Lewis-Williams's three stages of trance. Why does he consider entoptic phenomena a uniformitarian argument?

9. How does Lewis-Williams interpret the layout of images at Lascaux (Hall of the Bulls → Axial Gallery → Apse/Diverticule of the Felines) in terms of trance states?

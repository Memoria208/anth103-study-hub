## Big Picture: What This Chapter Is About

Chapter 9 is about how archaeologists reconstruct ancient diet, subsistence, and environments from the biological remains people left behind. Those remains fall into two broad categories: **faunal remains** (animal bones, studied by zooarchaeologists) and **plant remains** (studied by paleoethnobotanists). Neither kind of evidence speaks for itself — interpreting what it means requires the same middle-level theory introduced in Chapter 8: uniformitarianism, analogies, and taphonomy. The chapter walks through several interlocking methods — faunal analysis, palynology, phytolith analysis, lipid analysis, and wood rat midden studies — and two major case studies that show these methods doing real work: the Folsom-period Agate Basin site in Wyoming, and the Chavín de Huántar ceremonial center in Peru.

## Part 1: The Basics of Faunal Analysis

**Q: What is a faunal assemblage, and how does it differ from a paleontological assemblage?**
A: A faunal assemblage is the collection of animal bones recovered from an archaeological site where humans played some role in the accumulation. A paleontological assemblage, by contrast, contains animal remains that accumulated entirely through natural processes. The distinction matters because archaeological assemblages must be interpreted in terms of human behavior.

**Q: What two key questions must a zooarchaeologist address before interpreting behavior from bones?**
A: First, were the bones deposited by humans (rather than carnivores, raptors, or natural death)? Second, were they deposited during the same occupation of the site? Evidence used to answer these questions includes stone tool cut marks (vs. carnivore tooth marks), burning, impact fractures, association with hearths, anatomical position, and differential weathering.

**Q: What are the two steps in identifying an animal bone, and what does each require?**
A: The first step is assigning each specimen to an **element** — the anatomical body part (femur, rib, skull fragment, etc.). The second step is identifying it to **taxon** — the kind of animal, ideally to species. Both steps require hands-on familiarity with a **comparative collection** of modern animal skeletons.

**Q: What are animal size classes, and when are they used?**
A: When fragments are too small or ambiguous to identify to species or family, analysts assign them to one of five size classes based on body mass. Size class 1 = rodents and rabbits; 2 = wolf/pronghorn; 3 = mule deer/bighorn sheep; 4 = bison/elk; 5 = giraffes, hippos, elephants. It is the option of last resort — analysts always prefer species-level identification.

**Q: What is a comparative collection, and how is one built?**
A: A reference library of modern animal skeletons, labeled by species, age, sex, and collection data. Built by hunting, trapping, collecting road kills (with appropriate licenses), or acquiring carcasses from wildlife agencies. Specimens must be defleshed — by burial, simmering in detergent, or using dermestid beetle colonies — before they can be curated.

## Part 2: Counting Bones — NISP and MNI

**Q: What is NISP, and what is its main limitation?**
A: NISP (Number of Identified Specimens) is the total count of bone specimens identified to a particular taxon. It is easy to compute and good for comparing many assemblages across sites. Its limitation: a single highly fragmented skeleton could produce hundreds of NISP, inflating the apparent importance of that species relative to others.

**Q: What is MNI, and how is it calculated?**
A: MNI (Minimum Number of Individuals) is the minimum number of individual animals needed to account for all skeletal elements of a species. It is calculated by tabulating bone frequency by element and side (left vs. right), then taking the count of the most abundant element. Analysts compare fragments to check whether any could belong to the same individual, reducing the MNI accordingly.

**Q: What are the limitations of MNI?**
A: MNI depends heavily on how the site is divided: a coarse-grained, repeatedly occupied site might yield an MNI of 1 or 2 despite hundreds of fragments. It is also sensitive to fragmentation — highly broken bones are harder to match. MNI is most reliable in fine-grained assemblages with minimal fragmentation and fine stratigraphic divisions.

**Q: What is a fine-grained versus a coarse-grained assemblage?**
A: Fine-grained: the assemblage results from one or a few short occupations (like Agate Basin's Folsom component). Coarse-grained: the assemblage accumulated over many overlapping occupations across decades or centuries. MNI is most meaningful in fine-grained contexts.

## Part 3: The Agate Basin Case Study (Faunal Analysis in Action)

**Q: What did Matt Hill conclude about the Agate Basin Folsom component, and when was the site occupied?**
A: About 10,780 radiocarbon years ago, a small group of Folsom hunters camped at Agate Basin in late March or early April. They killed at least 11 *Bison antiquus* and 5 pronghorn antelope. Bison were partially butchered at the kill site; antelope were field-dressed and carried back nearly whole. The hunters appear to have lacked stored food and were hunting on a weekly or daily basis.

**Q: How did Hill determine the season of occupation?**
A: By analyzing tooth eruption and bone development in juvenile bison, which follow predictable developmental schedules. Two young animals were about 11 and 23 months old, placing their deaths in late March–early April (given that modern bison calve in late April–early May). Fetal bison bones corroborated a late-winter/early-spring occupation. This inference rests on a uniformitarian assumption: ancient bison birthing seasons closely approximated modern ones.

**Q: What does the pattern of missing axial skeleton elements at Agate Basin tell us?**
A: The axial skeleton (skull, vertebrae, ribs, sacrum) is rare compared to appendicular elements (limb bones). Since limb bones carry more meat and marrow than axial elements, this pattern indicates **selective transport**: hunters brought high-utility portions to camp and left low-utility axial elements at the kill site.

**Q: Why did Hill interpret the cracked toe bones (metapodials and phalanges) as evidence of food stress?**
A: Foot bones contain little marrow and have no meat. Processing them makes sense only when people are short on better food. Hill also notes that Folsom hunters seem not to have stored food — unlike later Plains hunter-gatherers — making the lean late winter/early spring period especially difficult.

## Part 4: Chavín de Huántar — Faunal Analysis and the Ch'arki Effect

**Q: What is Chavín de Huántar, and why is it a useful faunal case study?**
A: A major Andean ceremonial center (~3150–2450 BP) at 3150 m elevation in Peru. George Miller and Richard Burger analyzed ~2000 identified specimens across three temporal phases to track how subsistence changed from mixed hunting/herding to near-exclusive llama pastoralism, using both taxonomic and element-level data.

**Q: What are the four Andean camelids, and which are domesticated?**
A: Llama (domesticated; mainly used as a pack animal), alpaca (domesticated; valued for fine fiber), guanaco (wild; hunted for meat), vicuña (wild; hunted for fine hair). At Chavín de Huántar, llama and vicuña bones were most common.

**Q: What is the ch'arki effect, and why is it archaeologically important?**
A: Ch'arki is llama/alpaca meat freeze-dried on the bone in the high-altitude puna (the native Quechua word entered English as "jerky"). Head and foot bones are cut off during production and discarded at the puna herding site. The ch'arki chunks — with leg bones and vertebrae still attached — travel to valley trade partners. Result: puna sites accumulate head/foot bones; receiving sites accumulate leg/vertebra bones. Knowing this allows archaeologists to reconstruct trade networks from bone distributions alone.

**Q: How did the faunal assemblage at Chavín de Huántar change across the three phases?**
A: Urabarriu phase (earliest, 2950–2750 BP): mixed hunting and herding; camelid remains mostly head and foot bones, suggesting ch'arki was being produced locally or received from nearby puna. Chakinani phase (2750–2650 BP): deer drops off sharply; camelid rises to 83%. Janabarriu phase (2650–2350 BP): camelid at 93% of assemblage; leg bones now dominant — a clear ch'arki-receiving signature. The site had shifted from local self-sufficiency to long-distance trade dependence.

## Part 5: Plant Remains — Methods Overview

**Q: What are macrobotanical remains, and how are they recovered?**
A: Readily recognizable plant parts: seeds, charcoal, corncobs, nut hulls, etc. Best preserved in arid climates or when carbonized by fire. Recovered primarily through **flotation** (water separation of light plant material from heavy sediment), but also found in waterlogged contexts, adobe, coprolites, and ceramic interiors.

**Q: What is palynology, and why is pollen well preserved?**
A: Palynology is the analysis of ancient plant pollen and spores. Pollen grains are microscopic male gametes that plants shed in enormous quantities into the atmosphere. They are morphologically distinctive (identifiable to genus or species under a microscope) and chemically resistant — surviving hydrofluoric acid baths in the lab — allowing preservation for tens of thousands of years under the right conditions.

**Q: What is the sampling protocol for pollen in a stratigraphic profile?**
A: The profile face is scraped clean to expose fresh sediment. Samples of ~0.2–0.3 L are collected from bottom to top (not top to bottom) at 5–10 cm intervals to prevent contamination from falling dirt. Samples are sealed in sterile containers. In the lab, pollen is isolated via repeated hydrofluoric acid baths and centrifuging, mounted on slides, and scanned at 400–1000×.

**Q: How is a pollen diagram read, and what does it tell us?**
A: The diagram plots plant taxon percentages against stratigraphic levels, read from bottom (oldest) to top (most recent). Changes in pollen frequencies reflect changes in plant density and, by extension, past environments. You must distinguish local from regional signals: a spring in a desert may produce forest pollen that doesn't reflect regional vegetation.

**Q: What are phytoliths, and how do they differ from pollen as evidence?**
A: Phytoliths are microscopic silica bodies ("plant stones") deposited inside plant cells. Nearly indestructible, they persist for millions of years. Unlike pollen — which travels on wind and represents both local and regional plants — phytoliths are deposited where the plant grew or decayed, making them a highly **local** signal. Most useful for identifying grass and sedge species.

## Part 6: Case Studies in Plant Analysis

**Q: What did the three-part analysis at Stillwater Marsh (26CH1062, Nevada) conclude about bulrush seeds?**
A: Flotation recovered carbonized bulrush seeds (local food remains). Phytolith analysis found *no* sedge phytoliths — so no bulrush plants decayed on the site. Pollen analysis showed bulrush was abundant in the surrounding marsh. Conclusion: people brought bulrush seeds *to* the site as food, rather than using bulrush plants as building or craft material on-site.

**Q: What is the significance of the *absent* seeds at Stillwater Marsh?**
A: No upland plant seeds — piñon pine hulls, ricegrass — were found. This suggests people living in the Carson Desert obtained all their plant food from the wetland and did not travel even short distances into the foothills to gather upland resources.

**Q: What are wood rat (pack rat) middens, and what makes them valuable to paleoecology?**
A: Ancient Neotoma nests built in rock crevices and sheltered overhangs. Rats collect materials within ~100 m of the nest, so the assemblage represents a highly local sample. Urine lacquer on the nest preserves organic material — twigs, seeds, cones — for thousands of years. Radiocarbon dating of successive levels produces a detailed record of local vegetational change.

**Q: How did wood rat midden data from Nevada change the interpretation of Stillwater Marsh sites?**
A: Peter Wigand and Cheryl Nowak found that piñon pine evidence doesn't appear in middens near the Stillwater Range until ~1200 BP — *after* the marsh sites were occupied. So people weren't bypassing piñon in favor of marsh resources; piñon simply wasn't present in sufficient abundance to make a trip worthwhile. Environmental context is essential for interpreting subsistence choices.

**Q: What is lipid analysis, and what can it reveal?**
A: Extraction of fats, oils, and waxes (lipids) from pottery walls or sediments using chloroform/methanol, followed by gas chromatography and mass spectrometry. Because lipids absorb into ceramic walls during cooking and resist water, they survive long after other food traces disappear. Richard Evershed (University of Bristol) has identified vegetable, milk, and meat residues in European pottery and demonstrated that fat distribution within vessel walls matches the physics of cooking (fats rise to the top).

## Part 7: Beyond Economics — The Symbolic Meaning of Plants

**Q: What did Hastorf and Johannessen find in the Upper Mantaro Valley (Peru), and why is it significant?**
A: Analyzing charcoal from ~900 flotation samples spanning 1000 years (~1500–500 BP), they found that mature wood use declined through the Pancán phases (population growth → fuel scarcity) but then *increased* during Wanka II and III, against ecological expectations. Fuel management/tree cultivation seems to have been adopted — but not simply for economic reasons.

**Q: What symbolic role did trees play in Andean cosmology, and how does this complicate the economic explanation?**
A: Certain trees were planted at administrative sites and linked to deities. *Quishuar* wood was burned in rituals and used to carve human figures sacrificed to Inca ancestors. Trees symbolized family continuity, ancestors (roots), and children (fruit). A ritual step in marriage ceremonies was called "bringing the branch." Hastorf and Johannessen argue that the choice to plant trees — one of several solutions to fuel scarcity — reflects these cultural values, not just economic logic. This is a postprocessual insight: material evidence can carry symbolic meaning that purely ecological analysis misses.

## Key Terms Quick Reference

| Term | One-Line Definition |
|---|---|
| Ecofacts | Plant and animal remains in archaeological sites (not human-made) |
| Faunal assemblage | Animal bones recovered from a site; humans played some role in accumulation |
| Zooarchaeologist | Specialist in identifying and interpreting archaeological animal remains |
| Paleoethnobotanist | Specialist in plant remains and plant–people interactions |
| Element | Anatomical body part assigned to a bone specimen |
| Taxon | Biological classification assigned to a bone (ideally species) |
| Comparative collection | Reference skeletons of modern animals used for bone identification |
| NISP | Number of Identified Specimens; total count per taxon |
| MNI | Minimum Number of Individuals needed to account for all elements |
| Fine-grained assemblage | Assemblage from one or a few short, discrete occupations |
| Seasonality | The time of year a site was occupied |
| Agate Basin | Folsom-period site (~10,780 BP, Wyoming); key faunal analysis case study |
| Ch'arki effect | Faunal signature of freeze-dried llama jerky production vs. trade receiving |
| Chavín de Huántar | Andean ceremonial center; ch'arki effect case study |
| Macrobotanical remains | Visible plant parts (seeds, charcoal, etc.) recovered by flotation |
| Palynology | Analysis of ancient pollen and spores |
| Pollen diagram | Graph of pollen taxon percentages across stratigraphic levels |
| Phytoliths | Microscopic silica plant bodies; local signal, not regional |
| Wood rat middens | Preserved pack rat nests; record local vegetational history |
| Lipid analysis | Extraction of food residues from ceramics via gas chromatography |
| Usable meat value | MNI × average butchered weight; estimates relative dietary importance |

2. Walk through the evidence Matt Hill used to determine: (a) that humans deposited the Agate Basin bones, (b) that they were deposited during a single occupation, and (c) that the site was occupied in late winter/early spring.

3. What is the ch'arki effect? How does it explain the shift from head/foot bones to leg/vertebra bones across the three phases at Chavín de Huántar?

4. The Stillwater Marsh analysis used three different plant methods together. What did each one contribute, and why was all three necessary to reach the conclusion about bulrush?

5. Why did piñon pine absence in Stillwater Marsh sites initially seem like a behavioral choice, and what did wood rat middens reveal to change that interpretation? What does this tell us about interpreting subsistence data?

6. What is lipid analysis? What are its advantages over macrobotanical analysis for certain types of food evidence?

8. In your own words, explain why a zooarchaeologist must understand taphonomy before interpreting a faunal assemblage.

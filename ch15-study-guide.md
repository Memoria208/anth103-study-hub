## Big Picture: What This Chapter Is About

Chapter 15 asks a deceptively simple question: **what part of our past must we save, and what part can we do without?** Commercial development, looting, and time itself constantly threaten archaeological sites, so over the last century-plus the U.S. federal government has built a body of law to protect them — a legal framework generally known as **cultural resource management (CRM)**, which today accounts for about 90 percent of all field archaeology conducted in the United States. The chapter traces that legal history from private philanthropy in the 1880s through the **1906 Antiquities Act**, the **1966 National Historic Preservation Act (NHPA)** and its **Section 106**, and the **1979 Archaeological Resources Protection Act (ARPA)**. It then turns to a second, distinct body of law — the **1990 Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA)** — which shifts from *preserving* objects to *returning* them, and works through the **Kennewick Man** case as an extended illustration of how hard NAGPRA's key terms ("Native American," "cultural affiliation") are to apply. Finally, the chapter widens the lens to **international** looting and antiquities trafficking, and to the emerging threat of **climate change** to archaeological sites.

## Part 1: The Development of Cultural Resource Management

**Q: What is cultural resource management (CRM), and why does it matter so much to modern archaeology?**
A: CRM is **the field that conducts activities related to compliance with legislation that protects cultural resources**. Prior to the 1960s, nearly all American archaeologists worked for universities and museums; today, most archaeologists earn a living within the CRM framework, and CRM projects account for **about 90 percent** of the field archaeology conducted in the United States.

**Q: What were some of the earliest (pre-federal) efforts to protect American cultural heritage?**
A: Private citizens and organizations led the way well before Congress acted systematically. In **1789**, wealthy Bostonians formed the **Massachusetts Historical Society** after the destruction of John Hancock's house. In **1880**, the Archaeological Institute of America sent **Adolph Bandelier** to document looting at pueblo ruins in New Mexico. **Frederic Putnam** of Harvard's Peabody Museum saved Ohio's **Serpent Mound** in the 1880s simply by buying it (Harvard held title until 1900, when it transferred the site to the Ohio Historical Society). In 1889–1892, Boston philanthropists secured federal protection and funding for **Casa Grande** in Arizona, creating the nation's first archaeological "reservation."

**Q: How did America's environmental movement intersect with early historic preservation?**
A: Historic preservation developed as part of a broader conservation movement inspired by writers like Henry David Thoreau and John Muir, and by George Perkins Marsh's *Man and Nature* (1864). President **Theodore Roosevelt** was especially receptive to these ideas — he created the **U.S. Forest Service** in 1905 and, the following year, signed the Antiquities Act into law.

**Q: Who was Edgar Lee Hewett, and what was his role in the Antiquities Act?**
A: Hewett (1865–1946) was a mediocre archaeologist but a brilliant **administrator and lobbyist** who, as secretary of an American Anthropological Association committee, helped draft the first version of the Antiquities Act. He shrewdly framed the act around solving the specific problem of looting (rather than proclaiming a broad federal preservation policy), which made it more palatable to Congress.

## Part 2: Three Pillars of Federal Preservation Law

**Q: What are the three key provisions of the 1906 Antiquities Act?**
A:
1. It made it **illegal to excavate or collect** remains from archaeological sites on public lands **without a permit**.
2. Permits were restricted to **museums, universities, and other scientific/educational institutions**, with objects preserved permanently in public museums.
3. It gave the **president authority to create national monuments** on federal land containing historic or scientific objects of interest.

Roosevelt used the act immediately, designating **Devil's Tower** (Wyoming) and **Montezuma Castle** (Arizona) as the first national monuments in 1906; **Mesa Verde** became the first national park created specifically for its archaeological significance that same year.

**Q: What is the National Historic Preservation Act (NHPA) of 1966, and how is it different in character from the Antiquities Act?**
A: Where the Antiquities Act is short and simple, the **NHPA** is long, dense, and bureaucratic — but foundational to all archaeologists working in the U.S. today. It created a **systematic, nationwide** preservation program rather than the earlier piecemeal approach, establishing **State Historic Preservation Offices (SHPO)** and, later, **Tribal Historic Preservation Offices (THPO)**, along with the national **Advisory Council on Historic Preservation**.

**Q: What does Section 106 of the NHPA require, in plain language?**
A: If a project needs federal funding, licenses, or permits — an "undertaking" — regardless of whose land it's on, the responsible federal agency must **determine whether the project will adversely affect any site included in or eligible for the National Register** of Historic Places, and if so, must **mitigate** that impact (e.g., through excavation or rerouting). Section 106 does *not* mandate protecting sites at any cost — only that the government consider and weigh their value.

**Q: What four criteria determine whether an archaeological site is "significant" enough to be eligible for the National Register?**
A: A site is significant if it shows:
1. Association with events important to broad patterns of history, prehistory, or culture
2. Association with important people
3. Distinctive architectural, construction, or artistic characteristics
4. Known or likely potential to contain data important in history or prehistory (the criterion most commonly used by archaeologists)

**Q: What is the "Area of Potential Effect" (APE), and how do CRM surveys differ from research surveys?**
A: The **APE** is the area surveyed under Section 106 compliance — it includes not just the direct construction footprint but any area anticipated to be affected, including **viewsheds**. Unlike research surveys (where the research question shapes survey area), in **CRM surveys the construction project drives the survey area**, which can be oddly shaped (e.g., a 100-foot-wide, hundred-mile-long pipeline corridor) and is usually surveyed at **100 percent coverage** rather than sampled, since even rare, significant sites cannot be missed.

**Q: What did the 1979 Archaeological Resources Protection Act (ARPA) add that the Antiquities Act lacked?**
A: The Antiquities Act's penalties (a $500 fine, 90 days in prison) were far too weak for a modern illegal antiquities market where a single artifact can fetch tens of thousands of dollars — prior to 1979 there had been only **18 convictions** under the Antiquities Act. **ARPA** made it a **felony** to excavate, remove, damage, or deface archaeological resources on public or Indian lands without a permit, with penalties up to **$250,000 and/or 5 years in prison**, and also criminalized selling or transporting artifacts illegally removed from federal land. Surface collection of arrowheads was specifically exempted.

**Q: What happened in the Jack Lee Harelson / Elephant Mountain Cave case?**
A: Harelson looted a 10,000-year-old cave site on federal land in Nevada for years, stealing ancient sandals and desecrating the mummified remains of two children. He was assessed a **$2.5 million civil penalty under ARPA** in 2002, based on the professional cost to excavate and analyze what he destroyed. While awaiting appeal, he was caught trying to hire a hitman to kill five people (including the judge); he died in prison in 2012. The case illustrates both ARPA's civil-penalty mechanism and the broader point that looters are sometimes involved in other serious crime.

**Q: Does ARPA (or any federal law) protect sites on private land?**
A: No — with one important exception. **ARPA applies only to federal (and Indian) land.** On private land in the U.S. (unlike "treasure law" countries such as England or Mexico), whatever is found belongs to the landowner, and the government cannot confiscate it. The sole exception concerns **human burials**, which many states protect regardless of land ownership or age.

**Q: What was the Slack Farm incident, and what changed as a result?**
A: A Kentucky landowner leased his property to artifact miners who used shovels and bulldozers to loot a 15th-century Mississippian cemetery, disturbing more than **600 graves**. Because grave desecration was only a **misdemeanor** at the time (a $500 fine, 6-month maximum sentence), none of the ten looters were successfully prosecuted. Public outrage led Kentucky to **upgrade grave desecration to a felony**, including on private property.

## Part 3: International Looting and Repatriation

**Q: Why is the international antiquities trade compared to the drug trade?**
A: Because as long as buyers are willing to pay high prices, smugglers will supply the market — illegal antiquities trafficking ranks **just behind drugs and illegal arms** in international crime. Examples span the globe: Maya stelae cut apart for individual glyphs, Peruvian tombs stripped for gold, WWI graves robbed of medals, and thousands of Chinese graves looted annually despite steep penalties.

**Q: What international agreements and laws try to curb the antiquities trade?**
A: The **1970 UNESCO Convention** asks signatory nations to regulate import/export of cultural objects, forbid museums from acquiring illegally exported items, and establish art-dealer registries. The U.S. implemented this via the **1983 Cultural Property Implementation Act**. Some treaties "grandfather in" artifacts excavated before the treaty date. During wartime, the **1954 Hague Convention** obligates occupying forces to prevent looting of cultural sites; though the U.S. never ratified it, the U.S. military has generally abided by it (e.g., the Pentagon's 4,000-site "no-strike list" before the 2003 Iraq War).

**Q: What happened at the Baghdad Museum in 2003, and why does it matter?**
A: Despite promises to protect Iraqi cultural institutions, U.S. forces left the **Baghdad Museum unguarded** after entering the city on April 11, 2003. Looters destroyed documentation and stole many irreplaceable artifacts (though museum staff had moved much of the gold to secure vaults beforehand). The episode is a stark illustration of how quickly cultural heritage can be lost amid the chaos of war.

**Q: What is the central controversy over the Parthenon (Elgin) Marbles?**
A: **Lord Elgin** removed statues and portions of the Parthenon frieze from Ottoman-controlled Greece around 1800 and sold them to the British Museum in 1816. Greece argues Elgin exceeded his permission and that the marbles are core to Greek identity; Britain argues the acquisition was legal and that the marbles now belong to a shared "global patrimony." The debate raises the deeper question of **whether antiquities should be returned to their country of origin** — and whether doing so would trigger a cascade of claims worldwide.

**Q: How does global development create tension for sites like Machu Picchu?**
A: Peru, a poor nation, wants to build hotels, a high-speed railroad, and cable cars to boost tourism revenue near Machu Picchu, but geologists warn the proposed cable car towers sit in landslide-prone, structurally sensitive areas. UNESCO (which designated the site a World Heritage Site in 1983) opposes the cable cars. The case poses a recurring dilemma: **does the international community's stake in a "world heritage" site override a poor nation's sovereign right to develop it?**

**Q: How is climate change now threatening archaeological sites?**
A: Two major mechanisms: **(1) sea level rise**, which is projected to destroy tens of thousands of coastal sites (e.g., nearly 20,000 sites along the U.S. southeastern coast with just one meter of rise, according to modeling by David Anderson); and **(2) melting ice patches**, which have preserved organic artifacts (arrows, baskets, shoes) for thousands of years but now expose them to rapid decay as the ice recedes, creating a race against time for archaeologists.

## Part 4: NAGPRA and the Question of Native American Remains

**Q: What is NAGPRA, and how is its purpose different from the preservation laws discussed earlier?**
A: The **Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (1990)** moves cultural resource law from **preservation** into the realm of **human rights legislation** — its purpose is to facilitate the *return and reburial* of Native American human remains and certain cultural objects, not to keep them preserved in institutions. It was prompted in part by testimony that **43,306** individual Native American skeletons were held in 163 U.S. museums as of 1988, including remains taken from the 1864 Sand Creek Massacre.

**Q: What four categories of items does NAGPRA cover?**
A: (1) Human skeletal remains; (2) **funerary objects** (placed with a body during burial); (3) **sacred objects** (needed for current religious practice); and (4) **objects of cultural patrimony** (items of ongoing tribal importance that were inalienable — no individual had the right to give them away).

**Q: How does NAGPRA define "Native American," and why does that definition matter?**
A: NAGPRA defines Native American as **"of, or relating to, a tribe, people, or culture that is indigenous to the United States."** The definition matters because it uses the present tense ("is indigenous"), which courts have interpreted narrowly — meaning that great age alone does not automatically make remains "Native American" under the law if no relationship to a *present-day* tribe, people, or culture can be shown.

**Q: How does NAGPRA define "cultural affiliation," and why is it often harder to establish than "Native American" status?**
A: **Cultural affiliation** means "a relationship of shared group identity which can be reasonably traced historically or prehistorically between a present-day tribe...and an identifiable earlier group," determined by a preponderance of evidence (geographic, kinship, biological, archaeological, linguistic, oral tradition, etc.). It is often harder to prove than "Native American" status because tribes have migrated (e.g., the Athapaskan-speaking Navajo and Apache moved into the Southwest from northwestern Canada), formed through colonial-era changes (e.g., Plains horse culture after Spanish horses arrived), and changed culturally over centuries — all of which can break the traceable thread the law requires. Remains that cannot be affiliated are classed as **"unaffiliated."**

**Q: What happened when the Kennewick Man skeleton was discovered in 1996?**
A: Two teenagers found a skull along Washington's Kennewick River; forensic archaeologist **James Chatters** eventually recovered most of the skeleton and found a stone projectile point embedded in the hip. A radiocarbon date shocked everyone: the remains were **9,400 years old**, among the oldest ever found in the Americas. The media reported (based on a mischaracterization of Chatters's initial observation) that a "Caucasian" skeleton had been found. The Army Corps of Engineers moved to repatriate the remains to local tribes, but **eight archaeologists and biological anthropologists sued**, arguing the remains were not demonstrably Native American or culturally affiliated with any modern tribe.

**Q: How did the courts rule on whether Kennewick Man was "Native American," and on what reasoning?**
A: The judge ruled Kennewick Man was **not Native American** under NAGPRA, reasoning that the key word in the statutory definition is "is" (present tense) — age alone is not sufficient evidence, and the culture of a skeleton with no associated artifacts and an unknown burial context is "unknown and apparently unknowable." Additionally, Kennewick Man's cranial morphology more closely resembled that of Southeast Asians, Polynesians, or Japan's Ainu people than that of later Native Americans. The Ninth Circuit upheld this ruling in 2004. (Later aDNA analysis, mentioned in the chapter, placed Kennewick Man within the genetic spectrum of Native Americans — complicating, but not overturning, the legal ruling discussed here.)

**Q: Even if Kennewick Man were Native American, why did the court also rule he could not be culturally affiliated with the claimant tribes?**
A: With only a single burial and no grave goods, archaeologists could not identify Kennewick's social group at all — a baseline requirement for establishing cultural affiliation. Archaeological evidence also showed **major cultural change** in the Columbia Plateau over time (sedentary occupation didn't even begin until about 3,000 years ago, some 6,000 years after Kennewick Man died), undermining any claim of continuous "shared group identity." The judge also considered — but found insufficiently specific — oral traditions offered by the Umatilla and other Plateau tribes.

## Exam-Style Practice Questions

1. Explain the three key provisions of the 1906 Antiquities Act. Why was it strengthened by ARPA in 1979, and what specific weaknesses did ARPA address?

2. What is Section 106 of the NHPA, and how does the concept of an "Area of Potential Effect" differ from a research-driven survey area (as discussed in earlier chapters)?

3. Why does federal law protect archaeological sites so much more strongly on public land than on private land? Use the Slack Farm case to illustrate the consequences of this gap.

4. Compare the legal reasoning behind NAGPRA's definitions of "Native American" and "cultural affiliation." Why can a skeleton be extremely old and still not meet either definition?

5. Walk through the Kennewick Man case from discovery to final ruling. What specific evidence led the court to conclude the remains were not Native American, and separately, not culturally affiliated with the claimant tribes?

6. How does the international antiquities trade resemble the illegal drug trade, according to the chapter? What tools (treaties, laws) exist to combat it, and what are their limits?

7. Using the Parthenon Marbles or Machu Picchu case, discuss the tension between international/global interest in a heritage site and the sovereign rights of the country where it is located.

8. How is climate change altering the priorities of contemporary archaeology, and what historical precedent (discussed in this and earlier chapters) offers a model for responding to it?

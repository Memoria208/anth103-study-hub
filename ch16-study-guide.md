## Archaeology's Future
### ANTH 103 · Kelly & Thomas, *Archaeology* 7th ed.

## Big Picture: What This Chapter Is About

The final chapter closes the textbook by asking two forward-looking questions: **(1) how is archaeology relevant to the modern world**, beyond satisfying curiosity about the past, and **(2) how should archaeologists share control over knowledge of the past** with the many other communities who have a stake in it? The first half surveys **applied archaeology** — using archaeological methods to study modern garbage (the Garbage Project), assist criminal and forensic investigations, recover ancient technologies for modern use, and support public education. The second half turns to the ethics of **who owns the past**, using case studies (the Alamo, the Bighorn Medicine Wheel, Kennewick Man, Nazi archaeology, and Martin's Cove/Devils Tower) to explore the dangers of any single group — scientists or descendant communities alike — claiming exclusive authority over history. The chapter's closing argument is that archaeology's future depends on **dialogue among stakeholders as equal partners**, not a retreat into "pure science" or a surrender of scientific responsibility.

## Part 1: Applied vs. Pure Archaeology

**Q: What is the difference between "pure" and "applied" anthropology, and where does archaeology fit?**
A: **Pure** anthropology (and pure science generally) pursues knowledge for its own sake, driven by intellectual curiosity about large questions (how did humanity arise? nature vs. nurture?) without necessarily seeking practical application. **Applied** anthropology tries to put that knowledge to practical use — evaluating social programs, improving working conditions, or designing culturally appropriate health/agricultural interventions. Modern archaeology does both: this chapter focuses on ways archaeological **techniques** are applied to nontraditional problems and how knowledge of the human past addresses **concrete modern problems**.

**Q: What is the Garbage Project, and why did William Rathje start it?**
A: Begun by archaeologist **William Rathje** at the University of Arizona in **1973**, the Garbage Project applied archaeological methods to study **contemporary Tucson's trash**, on the premise (from Emil Haury) that "if you want to know what is really going on in a community, look at its garbage." Rathje was skeptical of questionnaire-based social research because respondents often misreport their own behavior — so instead of asking people what they consumed, his team **directly measured what they threw away**.

**Q: How did the Garbage Project reveal discrepancies between self-reported and actual behavior?**
A: When measuring beer consumption, garbage data showed **significantly heavier drinking** than household surveys reported. Low-income households tended to deny drinking any beer at all (fearing loss of welfare benefits), while middle-income households admitted to drinking but underreported quantity. The Garbage Project's findings gave later health researchers a way to **correct for this predictable skew** in self-reported survey data.

**Q: What major myths about American landfills did Rathje's excavations debunk?**
A: Contrary to popular belief, disposable diapers, plastic bottles, and fast-food containers together make up **less than 5–8 percent** of landfill volume. The single largest component is **paper** (40–50 percent), and — contrary to the assumption that landfills work like giant compost heaps — **very little actually biodegrades**: Rathje's team recovered 40-year-old newspapers still fully readable, because landfills lack the chopping, moisture, and turning that composting requires.

**Q: What is "forensic archaeology," and how did NYPD Sergeant McTigue come to use it?**
A: **Forensic archaeology** uses established archaeological excavation and recording techniques to assist law enforcement. Sergeant McTigue, investigating a series of NYC subway bombings, realized police weren't trained to systematically recover physical evidence from a blast site — so he consulted archaeologist David Hurst Thomas, who taught him grid systems, datum points, stratigraphic removal, and sifting/flotation. McTigue's team then used these techniques to recover the detonating device and make an arrest.

**Q: What did Phil DiBlasi's work at Louisville's Eastern Cemetery accomplish?**
A: DiBlasi used **backhoe trenches** and burial-capacity calculations to prove that Eastern Cemetery, established in 1843, had **exceeded its legal burial capacity** and that graves purchased by living people were already occupied — evidence of illegal **grave reuse dating to at least 1858**. His archaeological documentation (plan views, stratigraphy, artifact dating) was used to prosecute the cemetery's operators, even though the case was ultimately only a misdemeanor.

**Q: How have archaeologists contributed to investigations of mass atrocities and genocide?**
A: Archaeological teams have worked with United Nations tribunals to exhume and document mass graves from conflicts in **Rwanda, the former Yugoslavia (Croatia), and El Salvador**, among others. At Rwanda's Home Saint Jean site, archaeologists mapped, excavated, and analyzed remains from mass graves holding thousands of victims; cut-mark analysis on bones (e.g., defensive wounds on forearms) provided physical evidence that unarmed civilians were murdered as part of a genocide, evidence that supported UN tribunal indictments.

**Q: What role did archaeologists play after the September 11, 2001 World Trade Center attack?**
A: Brooklyn College archaeologist **Sophia Perdikaris** organized volunteer archaeologists to help search the debris (eventually hauled to the Fresh Kills Landfill) for human remains and personal effects, recognizing that archaeologists' skill at finding small things in a vast matrix of material was directly applicable. This effort inspired **Richard Gould** to found **Forensic Archaeology Recovery (FAR)**, a trained volunteer unit later deployed to the 2003 Station nightclub fire in Rhode Island, where archaeological method helped bring both comfort to victims' families and evidence for criminal prosecutions.

**Q: How are ancient agricultural technologies being revived to help modern farmers?**
A: Archaeologist **Clark Erickson** studied ancient **raised-field agriculture** around Peru/Bolivia's Lake Titicaca, where farmers (starting ~3,000 years ago) built raised planting mounds separated by canals that both irrigated crops and acted as heat sinks, reducing frost damage. Reintroducing this technology has shown **roughly double the potato yield** of conventional modern methods in some experiments, at lower capital cost — though adoption success varies by community and sociopolitical context.

**Q: Why do archaeologists invest in public education, and what is one example from the authors' own fieldwork?**
A: Because the public ultimately funds archaeological research, and educated publics are far less likely to loot or vandalize sites (and more likely to report those who do — as at Slack Farm, Chapter 15). At **Hidden Cave, Nevada** — a site repeatedly vandalized by looters — the excavation team ran daily public tours during fieldwork specifically to encourage the local community to "take possession" of and protect the site themselves, a strategy that has kept tours running for over 20 years.

## Part 2: Who Owns the Past?

**Q: What is the central ethical question posed by the second half of the chapter?**
A: Not "who owns a *site*" (a straightforward matter of property law), but **"who has the authority to uncover, interpret, and present knowledge gained from the past?"** This question becomes contentious wherever multiple groups — archaeologists, descendant communities, religious groups, tourists, governments — have a stake in the same place or the same story.

**Q: How does the controversy over the Alamo illustrate competing versions of history?**
A: The Alamo is popularly remembered for the 1836 battle (Travis, Bowie, Crockett), a cornerstone of Anglo-Texan founding mythology. But the site began as **Mission San Antonio de Valero**, an 18th-century Spanish mission — a chapter of history many Hispanic Texans feel has been suppressed. The **Daughters of the Republic of Texas (DRT)**, the site's state-appointed custodians, historically discouraged mission-period research because it "detracted" from the battle's significance; a newer generation of DRT members has since moved toward a more balanced interpretation.

**Q: What competing explanations have been proposed for the Bighorn Medicine Wheel, and why does the site remain contested today?**
A: Explanations range from **grave markers** commemorating warriors, to a site for **vision quests**, to astronomer John Eddy's argument that it functioned as a **solar/stellar observatory** aligned to the summer solstice (an argument later criticized for relying on too few confirmed sighting features). Regardless of its prehistoric function, the Wheel remains an active **sacred site** for many Native Americans today, creating tension between religious/ceremonial use and heavy tourist and researcher traffic — resolved in part by closing the final stretch of road to vehicle traffic.

**Q: How does William Tallbull's perspective differ from a strictly archaeological one?**
A: Tallbull, a Northern Cheyenne elder, described sacred sites not as "archaeological sites" but as places where **"our relatives lived and carried out their lives"** — living cultural sites still visited today for vision quests, healing, and connection to ancestors, approached with ritual protocol (e.g., stopping four times on the journey there). His account underscores that a site's scientific and spiritual meanings are not mutually exclusive, but can pull toward different priorities for access and interpretation.

**Q: What is Nazi archaeology, and why does the textbook treat it as a cautionary extreme?**
A: German prehistorian **Gustaf Kossinna** linked "Germanic" artifact types to territorial claims, work later appropriated by the Nazi regime to justify expansionist claims (including over Poland). Under Hitler, university archaeology chairs were funded only for enthusiastic Nazi Party members; **Heinrich Himmler's Ahnenerbe** unit searched for "Aryan" origins, and the **Sonderkommando Jankuhn** looted museums in conquered territory. Evidence was **fabricated or suppressed** to fit ideology, and dissenting scholars were silenced. The chapter treats this as the clearest historical warning of what happens when **one group gains total, unchecked control over interpreting the past.**

**Q: What happened with the LDS Church's attempt to acquire Martin's Cove, and what precedent concerned opponents?**
A: The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints sought to permanently purchase ~900 acres of BLM land at **Martin's Cove, Wyoming**, sacred to the church because 150 handcart pioneers died there in an 1856 blizzard. Opponents noted the land also contains **Native American archaeological sites and portions of the California Trail** used by many other groups, and warned that selling public sacred land to one religious group would set a precedent — for instance, could tribes then buy **Devils Tower**, sacred to many Native Americans but also a popular recreational climbing site? The Senate did not support the sale; the church instead accepted a 25-year lease extension in 2003.

**Q: What is the core argument on each side of the NAGPRA/reburial debate revisited in this chapter?**
A: Critics (some archaeologists) argue that reburial **permanently removes irreplaceable scientific data** from the public domain and constitutes a "tyranny of the minority," comparing forced silence about it to censorship — some go so far as an (contested) analogy to Nazi book-burning. Supporters (many archaeologists and Native Americans) counter that **human remains are not ordinary data** but the last vestige of a once-living person, and that scientific claims to universal access can themselves function as a form of colonialism ("they took our land, and now they've come for our dead"). The textbook authors conclude that **science should not automatically trump all other ethical concerns**, though NAGPRA itself is only legally concerned with proper procedure, not with settling the ethical debate.

**Q: What is the authors' own resolution to the "who owns the past" question?**
A: They argue archaeology cannot retreat to claiming the "high moral ground of pure science" while ignoring how other communities find meaning in the past — doing so would alienate the very publics whose support archaeology depends on. At the same time, **abandoning scientific standards entirely** would leave the field of interpretation open to unchecked, unfalsifiable claims (e.g., the Asatru Folk Assembly's claim to Kennewick Man, or Erich von Däniken's ancient-astronaut theories, discussed in Chapter 8). Their proposed middle path is genuine **dialogue among equal partners** — archaeologists and stakeholder communities working jointly, neither side abdicating its role.

## Exam-Style Practice Questions

1. Using the Garbage Project as an example, explain why Rathje distrusted questionnaire-based social research and what archaeological methods offered instead. What specific myth about landfills did his excavations overturn?

2. Compare two examples of "applied archaeology" from this chapter (e.g., forensic archaeology, disaster response, ancient agricultural technology). What do they suggest about archaeology's practical value beyond understanding the past?

3. What does the chapter mean by asking "who owns the past"? Explain why this is a different question from asking who legally owns a site.

4. Use the Alamo case study to explain how the same physical site can support multiple, competing historical narratives, and how those narratives can serve different political interests today.

5. Why does the textbook use Nazi archaeology as an extreme cautionary example? What specific mechanisms (funding, propaganda, suppression of dissent) allowed archaeology to be manipulated for political ends?

6. Summarize both sides of the debate over NAGPRA and reburial as presented in this chapter. According to the authors, should scientific value automatically outweigh other ethical concerns? Why or why not?

7. What precedent concerned opponents of the LDS Church's attempt to purchase Martin's Cove, and how does the Devils Tower comparison illustrate that concern?

8. In the authors' own words, what is the "right" way for archaeologists to balance scientific authority with the interests of descendant and stakeholder communities? Do you find their proposed solution convincing?

# Chapter 13 Study Guide
## Understanding Key Transitions in World Prehistory
### ANTH 103 · Kelly & Thomas, *Archaeology* 7th ed.

## Big Picture: What This Chapter Is About

Chapter 13 steps back from case studies to ask a bigger methodological question: how do archaeologists explain large-scale transitions in human prehistory — like the shift from foraging to farming, or from small egalitarian societies to centralized states? Because archaeology is a **historical science**, it can't run controlled experiments the way a laboratory science can; instead, it has to reason from **necessary** and **sufficient conditions** to reconstruct why particular changes happened when and where they did. The chapter traces the discredited 19th-century idea of a single, universal ladder of cultural "progress" (unilineal evolution), then works through the competing theories proposed to explain two of prehistory's biggest transitions: the origins of agriculture (plant domestication) and the rise of the archaic state. The Maya case study closes the chapter as an example of state formation viewed through multiple causal lenses at once.

---

## Part 1: Historical Science and Necessary/Sufficient Conditions

**Q: Why can't archaeology use the same method as a laboratory science?**
A: Laboratory sciences can repeat experiments, changing one variable at a time while holding everything else constant, to isolate what effect that variable has. Archaeology, as a **historical science**, studies events that happened once, in the past, and can't be rerun — so it has to reconstruct cause and effect from the surviving material record instead of controlled experiment.

**Q: What are necessary and sufficient conditions, and why do they matter for explaining prehistoric change?**
A: **Necessary conditions** must exist for a particular change to occur, but their presence alone doesn't guarantee the change will happen. **Sufficient conditions** are the minimal conditions needed to actually produce the change. Archaeologists explaining a transition (like the origin of agriculture) have to identify not just factors that were present, but which combination of factors was actually sufficient to cause the shift — since many "necessary" conditions (e.g., a suitable climate) existed for a long time before farming actually began.

## Part 2: Evolutionary Studies — From Unilineal Evolution to Modern Evolutionism

**Q: What is unilineal cultural evolution, and why did anthropology reject it?**
A: The belief that all human societies evolve culturally along a **single developmental trajectory**, with different societies simply occupying different rungs on the same ladder. These schemes typically depicted Western civilization as the most "advanced" stage — a Eurocentric and ethnocentric framework anthropology has rejected.

**Q: What is the comparative method?**
A: An Enlightenment-era idea holding that the world's existing (contemporary) peoples reflect different stages of human cultural evolution — i.e., that "primitive" living societies are frozen snapshots of Europe's own ancestral past.

**Q: How did Charles Darwin's work influence 19th-century social thought?**
A: Darwin (1809–1882) published *On the Origin of Species* in 1859, describing **natural selection** — the process by which some individuals survive and reproduce at higher rates than others because of their genetic heritage, perpetuating certain genetic traits at the expense of others. This biological theory was quickly (and wrongly) extended to explain human social and cultural differences.

**Q: What did John Lubbock argue, and what assumption underlies his work?**
A: In *Pre-historic Times* (1865), Lubbock illustrated the lives of "paleolithic" and "neolithic" peoples by comparing them to contemporary "primitives" — native peoples of Africa, Asia, and Australia — on the assumption that these living groups were essentially frozen approximations of what ancient Europeans used to be. (Lubbock also coined the term "Neolithic.")

**Q: What is Social Darwinism, and what did it claim?**
A: The extension of Darwinian evolutionary principles to social phenomena. It implied that conflict between societies, and between classes within a society, benefits humanity by weeding out "unfit" individuals and social forms. Social Darwinism assumed unfettered economic competition and warfare were the primary ways to determine which societies were "fittest," grounding cultural differences in claimed biological differences.

**Q: How did Franz Boas push back against unilineal evolution and Social Darwinism?**
A: Boas (1858–1942) identified **ethnocentrism** — the belief that one's own cultural ways are superior to any other — as the flaw underlying these schemes, and proposed **historical particularism** instead: the view that each culture is the product of its own unique sequence of developments, in which chance plays a major role in bringing about change (rather than all cultures marching through the same fixed stages).

**Q: How does modern evolutionary thinking in anthropology differ from 19th-century unilineal evolutionism?**
A: Modern evolutionism contains none of the racist or moral overtones of unilineal evolutionism. It recognizes that if natural selection operates on cultural phenomena at all, it works far more subtly than it does among animals. By contrast, unilineal evolutionists — however much they argued over details — all believed in a single, immutable developmental sequence that every society was assumed to pass through.

## Part 3: The Generalized Historical Sequence

Kelly & Thomas summarize a generalized (not universal, not unilineal) sequence of societal types often used as a comparative framework: **band → tribe → chiefdom → state**. Each stage differs along several dimensions:

| Dimension | Band | Tribe | Chiefdom | State |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Subsistence** | Foraging | Foraging, horticulture, pastoralism | Agriculture; pastoralists often incorporated | Agriculture, industry; pastoral specialists separated |
| **Economic organization** | Equal access to resources via sharing/reciprocity | Reciprocity; limited redistribution by charismatic leaders | Chief redistributes goods; some non-food producers | Elites control strategic resources; many non-food producers |
| **Political organization** | Egalitarian | Egalitarian; temporary authority roles; competitive feasting for rank | Status tied to genealogical closeness to a chief with a permanent, inherited office | Controlled by elites, run by specialists |
| **Settlement pattern** | Temporary camps, some reoccupied seasonally | Sedentary villages (temporary camps among pastoralists) | Sedentary villages of ranked sizes | Hierarchy of settlements reflecting administration; may include cities |
| **Population density** | Low | Low to medium | Medium to high | High |

**Q: What is a tribal society, and how does it differ from a band?**
A: **Tribal societies** cover a wide range of social formations between egalitarian foragers and ranked societies like chiefdoms. They're normally horticultural and sedentary, with a higher level of competition than seen among nomadic hunter-gatherers.

**Q: What defines an archaic state, and how does it relate to "civilization"?**
A: An **archaic state** is a centralized political system found in complex societies, characterized by having a virtual monopoly on the power to coerce. **Civilization** is a complex urban society with a high level of cultural achievement in the arts and sciences, craft specialization, a surplus of food and/or labor, and a hierarchically stratified social organization.

**Q: What's the difference between horticulture and intensive agriculture?**
A: **Horticulture** is cultivation using hand tools only, where plots are used for a few years and then left fallow. **Intensive agriculture** uses draft animals, machinery, or hand tools on plots farmed annually, and often involves irrigation, land reclamation, and fertilizers.

## Part 4: Why Were Plants Domesticated?

The chapter presents a series of competing theories for the origin of agriculture, moving roughly from purely environmental to purely social explanations.

**Q: What is Oasis Theory (V. Gordon Childe)?**
A: Argues that domestication arose as people, plants, and animals congregated around water sources (oases) during the arid years that followed the Pleistocene. In this scenario, agriculture arose because of "some genius" and preceded animal domestication.

**Q: What is Hilly Flanks Theory (Robert Braidwood)?**
A: Claims agriculture arose in the specific areas where wild ancestors of domesticated wheat and barley naturally grow. It attributes agriculture's appearance to human efforts to increase the productivity and stability of the existing food base, combined with a culture being "ready" to accept an agricultural lifeway.

**Q: What is the Density-Equilibrium Model (Lewis Binford)?**
A: Attributes the origins of agriculture to **population pressure** in favorable environments, which pushed some groups to emigrate into marginal lands — where agriculture became necessary to increase productivity. Population pressure is the result of a population reaching **carrying capacity** (the number of people a unit of land can support under a particular technology).

**Q: What is Optimal Foraging Theory, and what does it predict about the shift to agriculture?**
A: The idea that foragers select foods that maximize their overall **return rate** (the energy acquired per unit of harvesting/processing time). It predicts that as high-return-rate resources (like large game animals) become scarce, foragers add lower-return-rate resources (like seeds) to their diet — a shift that can set the stage for cultivation.

**Q: What is coevolution, and how does David Rindos apply it to plant domestication?**
A: **Coevolution** is an evolutionary theory arguing that changes in social systems are best understood as *mutual* natural selection among components, rather than a simple linear cause-and-effect sequence. David Rindos argued that because of certain plants' genetic composition and how they must be harvested, the very act of harvesting them resulted in **unintentional selection** — the plants gradually became dependent on humans for survival, without anyone "inventing" agriculture on purpose.

**Q: What is Brian Hayden's prestige-based theory?**
A: Hayden suggests agriculture arose as a way to increase productivity so that certain individuals could garner **prestige and power** — new technologies let hunter-gatherers harvest resources more efficiently and build food surpluses, which ambitious individuals could then leverage socially.

**Q: What's the common thread across these paradigms, and what caution does the chapter raise?**
A: Each paradigm privileges some combination of human intent, environment, demography, and selection to explain agriculture's origins — but population and environment may not be the only relevant variables at work.

## Part 5: The Origins of Agriculture in the Near East

**Q: What and where is the Fertile Crescent?**
A: A broad arc of mountains running through Israel, Jordan, Syria, Iraq, and Iran, where wild wheat, barley, and other plants that were later domesticated are still found growing today.

**Q: What was the Younger Dryas, and why does it matter to agricultural origins?**
A: A climatic interval (roughly 13,000–11,600 BP) marked by a rapid return to cooler, drier, but highly variable climatic conditions — occurring after a warming trend, and disrupting the resource base of people who had begun to settle down.

**Q: Who were the Natufians, and what makes them significant?**
A: A cultural manifestation in the **Levant** (the southwest Fertile Crescent), dating from about 14,500 to 11,600 BP. The Natufians are associated with the first appearance of settled villages, trade goods, and possibly early cultivation of domesticated wheat — but they still lacked pottery.

**Q: When did full-time agriculture actually begin in the Near East, and why is that date hard to pin down?**
A: It's difficult to distinguish wild wheat and barley from early domesticated forms in the archaeological record, so archaeologists can't identify an exact start date for intentional agriculture. The best current evidence suggests a full-time agricultural economy began roughly 10,000 to 9,000 BP.

**Q: What is the Neolithic, and who coined the term?**
A: The ancient period during which people began using ground stone tools, manufacturing ceramics, and relying on domesticated plants and animals — literally the "New Stone Age." The term was coined by Sir John Lubbock in 1865. Ofer Bar-Yosef estimates that by about 9,000 years ago, a growing population required that the full productivity of agriculture be harnessed, and true agricultural communities appeared.

**Q: How do the competing paradigms play out when applied specifically to the Near East?**
A: Late Pleistocene foragers already understood that seeds produce plants, but that knowledge alone isn't sufficient to explain why agriculture began when it did. V. Gordon Childe emphasized climatic change — foragers couldn't become farmers without a supportive environment, which didn't arrive until the early Holocene, and the Younger Dryas specifically disrupted Natufian foraging lifeways, pushing a formerly sedentary population toward cultivation to make the land more productive. Evidence from Natufian settlements also shows population growth pushing some groups to settle near stands of wild wheat and barley, with population pressure requiring people to broaden their diets and rely more heavily on plants. Finally, agriculture may have enabled feasts that helped form the social alliances critical to survival during the climatically uncertain Younger Dryas — meaning humans intentionally sought to increase productivity and improve their foraging success, which led them toward agriculture. In short: climate, demography, and human intent all appear to have worked together rather than any single factor acting alone.

## Part 6: Why Did the Archaic State Arise?

**Q: What is "the state," structurally?**
A: A strong, usually highly centralized government with a professional ruling class, largely divorced from the bonds of kinship that characterize simpler societies. Residential patterns in a state are based on occupational specialization rather than blood or **affinal** (marriage-based) relationships.

**Q: What is the Irrigation Hypothesis (Karl Wittfogel)?**
A: Attributes the origin of the state to the administrative demands of large-scale irrigation. The need for coordinated labor and massive construction projects led to increased wealth and military strength, and eventually to the powerful ruling bureaucracy characteristic of state development.

**Q: What is the Warfare and Circumscription Hypothesis (Robert Carneiro)?**
A: Attributes the origin of the state to the administrative burden of warfare conducted for conquest — a response to geographic limits on arable land (circumscription) in the face of a rising population. Carneiro's premise is that political change of lasting significance arises only from coercive pressure, and that warfare is the only mechanism powerful enough to impose bureaucratic authority on a large scale.

**Q: What is the Multicausal Theory of state formation (Allen Johnson & Timothy Earle)?**
A: Lists three conditions necessary for archaic states to form: (1) high population density that strains the food production system, (2) a need for a system of integration, and (3) the possibility of controlling the economy to permit financing of institutions and support a ruling class. Given these conditions, any number of specific historical processes can result in a state — this theory doesn't commit to one single trigger.

**Q: What role does ideology play in state formation?**
A: **Ideology** is a set of beliefs — often political, religious, or cosmological — that rationalizes exploitive relations between classes or social groups. In states, ideologies are frequently linked to religion, with elites tying themselves to deities; once these ideologies take on a life of their own, they can play a genuinely causal role in the development and maintenance of states, not just a decorative one.

## Part 7: The Maya — A Case Study of State Formation

**Q: How did Maya society develop from its earliest horticultural roots to statehood?**
A: Maize horticulturalists moved into the Maya region about 4,000 years ago, and evidence suggests these early farmers were **egalitarian**. During the **Middle Preclassic Period** (2900–2300 BP), population increase, trade, and sedentary slash-and-burn horticulture precipitated a change toward small **chiefdoms**.

**Q: What role did ancestor worship play?**
A: **Ancestor worship** — a religion in which one's deceased ancestors serve as important intermediaries between the natural and supernatural worlds — was part of the ideological framework accompanying this social change.

**Q: What happened during the Late Preclassic Period?**
A: (2300–1750 BP) Regional population grew by roughly 350% and aggregated into large settlements. Political organization at this point had no single center; instead, many small centers each controlled the territory around them.

**Q: What characterizes the Classic Period, and what pressures led to the emergence of a true archaic state?**
A: The **Classic Period** (1750–1300 BP) is characterized by the archaic state. High population density placed pressure on the agricultural economy and, in dry years, could lead to conflict. Warfare required social integration, as did the need to maintain the flow of goods and information between allied centers. As one large center likely gained an edge in authority, families may have moved to it, with smaller centers offering their allegiance in exchange for protection — a plausible mechanism for how centralized Maya states consolidated power.

## Exam-Style Practice Questions

1. Why can't archaeology test hypotheses about prehistory the way a laboratory science tests hypotheses? How do necessary and sufficient conditions help fill that gap?

2. What is unilineal cultural evolution, and what specific criticisms (from Boas and others) led anthropology to reject it?

3. Compare Oasis Theory, Hilly Flanks Theory, and the Density-Equilibrium Model as explanations for the origin of agriculture. What kind of factor (environmental, demographic, social) does each emphasize?

4. Explain David Rindos's coevolutionary argument for plant domestication. How does it differ from theories that emphasize deliberate human intent?

5. What is the Younger Dryas, and how did it affect Natufian lifeways in the Fertile Crescent?

6. Compare the Irrigation Hypothesis, the Warfare and Circumscription Hypothesis, and the Multicausal Theory as explanations for the rise of the archaic state. What is the key difference between a single-cause theory and a multicausal one?

7. Using the Maya case study, trace the sequence from egalitarian horticulturalists to a fully formed archaic state. What role did population growth and warfare play at each stage?

8. What is the difference between sufficient and necessary conditions in an explanation, and why does this distinction matter when comparing competing theories of agricultural or state origins?

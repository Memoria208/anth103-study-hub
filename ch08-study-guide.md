## Big Picture: What This Chapter Is About

Archaeologists can excavate and date sites, but how do they actually know what people *did* in the past? Chapter 8 answers that question. The key is **middle-level research** — a set of methods that build the bridge between a raw artifact and a behavioral inference. The three methods covered here are taphonomy, experimental archaeology, and ethnoarchaeology. All three are grounded in the **principle of uniformitarianism**: natural and behavioral processes work the same way today as they did in the past, so we can use modern observations to interpret ancient remains.

---

## Part 1: Middle-Level Theory

**Q: What is middle-level theory, and why does archaeology need it?**
A: Middle-level theory is the body of knowledge that connects observable archaeological data to inferences about past human behavior. Without it, interpretations would be little more than guesswork. Lewis Binford called for its development in the 1970s because facts — artifacts, bones, features — cannot explain themselves. Archaeologists need principled reasons to say "this pattern means that behavior."

**Q: What are bridging arguments?**
A: Logical "if…then" statements that translate a hypothesis into testable predictions. Example: *If* Plains Indians were rendering bone grease at a site, *then* we should find specific artifact types, bone distributions, and missing skeletal elements. They are essential to hypothesis testing and among the hardest things archaeologists construct.

**Q: Where did the concept of uniformitarianism come from?**
A: Scottish farmer and geologist **James Hutton (1726–1797)** formulated it by observing geological processes on his land. His insight: processes that shape the earth today (glaciation, erosion, sedimentation) are identical to past processes. Geologists — and then archaeologists — borrowed this logic. If modern glaciers leave striations and moraines, then ancient striations and moraines mean past glaciers.

**Q: What is analogy in archaeology, and what are the two types?**
A: An analogy infers that an archaeological object or feature had the same function as a similar ethnographic one.
- **Formal analogy**: based on similarities in form alone, regardless of cultural connection. Strengthened by many ethnographic examples and many shared attributes.
- **Relational analogy**: requires a cultural or environmental relationship between the archaeological and ethnographic cases, OR a causal/necessary link between attributes. Stronger and closer to middle-level theory.

**Q: What makes a relational analogy become middle-level theory?**
A: When it explains *why* a relationship between behavior and material remains must be true — not just that similar patterns exist elsewhere, but that a causal mechanism (anatomy, physics, human decision-making) requires the relationship.

## Part 2: Taphonomy

**Q: What is taphonomy and who coined the term?**
A: The study of how natural, noncultural processes contribute to the formation of archaeological sites — especially how organisms (bones, plants) become incorporated into and altered within the record. The term was coined by Russian paleontologist **I.A. Efremov** in the 1940s (from the Greek for "laws of burial").

**Q: What kinds of things do taphonomists study?**
A: How large carcasses decompose on savannas; what marks lion kills versus human kills; what rodent bones look like after passing through raptors; how wind and water carry seeds, pollen, and sediment. Taphonomy is about documenting and explaining natural processes so their effects can be separated from human ones.

**Q: Why is it useful to "remove" natural patterns from an archaeological site?**
A: Because what's left after accounting for natural processes must be explained by human behavior. This two-step logic helps archaeologists isolate behavioral patterns from noise.

**Q: What is the natural sequence of skeletal disarticulation? (Hill & Behrensmeyer)**
A: In large animals, joints separate in a predictable order driven by anatomy (cartilage, tendon, and muscle):
1. Scapula–vertebral column (front limb drops away first)
2. Caudal vertebrae–sacrum joint
3. Scapula–humerus joint
4. "Elbow" (humerus–radius/ulna)
5. Vertebrae disarticulate last

**Q: What is the Hudson-Meng Bison Bonebed, and why is it a key taphonomy case study?**
A: A site in northwest Nebraska (~9,500 radiocarbon BP) containing remains of at least 500 bison in ~1,000 sq. m., with 21 projectile points found among them.

- **Agenbroad's interpretation (1970s)**: Missing tops of bison skulls → analogy with Plains Indian brain extraction for hide tanning → humans killed the animals → implied a buried cliff, large group, sophisticated food storage.
- **Todd & Rapson's reanalysis**: Applied taphonomy. No cut marks. Carnivore tooth marks present. Many bones in anatomical position. Only 21 points (not the ~150 expected for 500 bison). All inconsistent with human butchery.

**Q: How does taphonomy explain the missing bison skull tops at Hudson-Meng?**
A: A decomposing carcass collapses into a flat pile that traps blowing sediment. It takes only 10–15 cm of sediment to bury limb bones, but 30–40 cm to cover the skull. The skull top stays exposed longer → sunlight destroys bone → the exposed top flakes away and disappears. This is middle-level theory because it explains *why* (anatomy, physics of sediment accumulation, UV bone destruction) not just *that* the pattern occurs.

**Q: What does Todd & Rapson hypothesize actually happened at Hudson-Meng?**
A: A summer prairie fire may have driven the bison herd into the low swale for shelter, then jumped the swale and asphyxiated the animals by consuming available oxygen. The few projectile points were likely lost or discarded by later visitors and moved downward through postdepositional processes.

## Part 3: Experimental Archaeology

**Q: What is experimental archaeology, and when is it especially useful?**
A: Controlled experiments that replicate past behaviors to identify links between behavior and its material consequences. Most useful when no living society performs the behavior (e.g., making Folsom points, building pyramids with ancient techniques) or when mechanical relationships need to be established.

**Q: Who was Ishi, and why does he matter to experimental archaeology?**
A: Ishi (c. 1860–1916) was the last known member of the Yahi people, found near Oroville, California in 1911. He was brought to UC San Francisco, where surgeon **Saxton Pope** learned archery from him. After Ishi's death (from tuberculosis), Pope continued experimenting with ancient bows and arrows, publishing *Hunting with the Bow and Arrow* (1923) — an early and influential work of experimental archaeology that provided baseline data for interpreting ancient archery equipment.

**Q: What did Don Crabtree discover about Folsom point fluting?**
A: Crabtree spent 40 years replicating the distinctive channel flakes ("flutes") on Folsom points (~10,800 BP). He tested 11+ methods; most failed. He eventually succeeded using a **chest crutch** (based on a 1615 description by Spanish friar Juan de Torquemada) to press off channel flakes while the point was held in a vise between his feet. Key findings:
- Fluting is extremely difficult regardless of technique
- High breakage rate near end of manufacturing sequence
- Fluting appears to have no specific functional advantage (unfluted points of the same era were just as effective)

**Q: What does experimental archaeology actually prove?**
A: Usually, it proves what *could* have happened — that a technique was not impossible. Pinpointing the *exact* technique used requires additional comparison of ancient and experimental artifacts and waste flakes. The uniformitarian contribution is telling us what the laws of physics and fracture mechanics permit or exclude.

**Q: How did Mark Lehner use experimental archaeology to test the pyramid "alien hypothesis"?**
A: Lehner built a small pyramid using only dirt ramps (7% incline), wooden sledges, log rollers, rope, and human labor. A 2-ton stone could be moved by 20 workers up the ramp; 4–5 workers with levers could position it on top. He completed the pyramid in 3 weeks. Conclusion: the physical principles of levers, wedges, and ramps are unchanged since ancient Egypt — no alien intervention required.

**Q: What is microwear analysis?**
A: The study of minute wear patterns on stone tool edges (striations, polish, pitting, microflaking) to infer how tools were used and what materials they worked.
- **Low-power (Tringham)**: 40–60× stereoscopic microscope; focuses on microflaking. Cutting = even scars both sides; scraping = scars on opposite face only; boring = trapezoidal scars on sides.
- **High-power (Keeley)**: up to 400× magnification; focuses on micropolishes. Different materials (bone, wood, hide, plant) produce distinct polishes. Blind tests validated the approach.

## Part 4: Ethnoarchaeology

**Q: What is ethnoarchaeology, and why is it harder to apply uniformitarianism in it?**
A: The study of living societies to see how human behavior produces material remains, providing analogies and middle-level theory for archaeological interpretation. Uniformitarianism is harder here because human behavior is not mechanical — cultural ideas, social norms, and situational factors make behavior less predictable than geological or physical processes.

**Q: Why did Lewis Binford go to Alaska to study people when his research interest was Neanderthals in France?**
A: Binford needed a place where he could *observe* human behavior and its material consequences independently — not infer behavior from remains as he had to do in archaeology. The Nunamiut's Arctic environment and large-game hunting offered rough parallels to the French Middle Paleolithic. His real goal was testing the concepts archaeologists use, not the Nunamiut themselves.

**Q: What did Binford find among the Nunamiut Eskimo?**
A: The *same individuals* left very different assemblages at different locations depending on season, distance from camp, available transport, amount of food already stored, and weather. This demonstrated that differences in artifact assemblages cannot automatically be attributed to cultural differences — site function and other variables must first be ruled out.

**Q: What were the Mikea studies, and what do they reveal archaeologically? (Kelly, Madagascar)**
A: Kelly studied four Mikea settlement types to see if length of occupation is reflected in material remains:

| Settlement | Trash Distance | Post Variability | Feature Diversity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Permanent village | 10–40 m | Low | High |
| Forest hamlet | 4–9 m | Low–Medium | Medium |
| Seasonal hamlet | 3–4 m | High | Medium–Low |
| Foraging camp | 1–2 m | n/a | Low |

Key patterns: the longer a settlement is occupied, the farther trash is deposited, the more consistent post diameters are, and the greater the diversity of features. These are all archaeologically recoverable.

**Q: Why do Mikea trash deposits end up farther from houses over time?**
A: Early in a settlement's life, nearby bushes catch thrown trash. As those bushes are destroyed (by children, livestock, and firewood gathering), trash is swept farther out. In long-occupied hamlets, periodic cleanings eventually create a second trash ring around the entire settlement.

**Q: How does human behavioral ecology support the Mikea findings as more than just analogy?**
A: The theoretical framework holds that people maximize the utility of their time. Building a wattle-and-daub house (1+ month) makes no sense for a camp that will be used for only a few weeks. This economic logic explains *why* labor investment scales with intended permanence — moving the inference toward middle-level theory rather than mere formal analogy.

**Q: How does Ian Hodder's Sudan research complicate trash-based inferences?**
A: Hodder found that the Moro physically remove trash from their compounds, while the Mesakin leave trash in courtyards and use ritual painting to achieve symbolic "cleanliness." This shows that cultural ideas about pollution and purification can override the simple occupation-length pattern. Archaeologists must be cautious: physical absence of trash doesn't always mean short occupation.

**Q: How do the Mikea data apply to the Mimbres Valley case (Nelson & Hegmon)?**
A: In southern New Mexico, Classic Mimbres phase (1000–850 BP) field houses had few posts, minimal features, and light construction — comparable to Mikea seasonal hamlets. In the Post-Classic phase (850–700 BP), those same sites gained more posts, masonry walls, and greater feature diversity — comparable to Mikea village construction. Nelson & Hegmon concluded people didn't abandon the region; they shifted from large pueblos to dispersed farmsteads, converting field houses into permanent residences.

## Key Terms Quick Reference

| Term | One-Line Definition |
|---|---|
| Middle-level theory | Bridges behavior and observable archaeological data |
| Uniformitarianism | Past processes = present processes (Hutton) |
| Bridging argument | "If…then" statement linking hypothesis to testable prediction |
| Taphonomy | Study of natural processes forming the archaeological record (Efremov) |
| Experimental archaeology | Controlled replication of past behaviors |
| Ethnoarchaeology | Study of living societies to build archaeological analogies |
| Formal analogy | Similarity in form between archaeological and ethnographic cases |
| Relational analogy | Archaeological and ethnographic cases are culturally/causally related |
| Schlep effect | Perkins & Daly: lower limb bones dragged home; upper limb bones left at kill |
| Hudson-Meng | 500 bison; taphonomy explains missing skull tops, not human butchery |
| Microwear | Microscopic wear patterns on stone tool edges (Semenov, Tringham, Keeley) |
| Nunamiut research | Binford: same people, different assemblages by season/function |
| Mikea settlements | Kelly: permanence → farther trash, consistent posts, more features |
| Human behavioral ecology | People maximize utility of time; explains labor investment in housing |

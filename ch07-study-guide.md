## Core Concepts

**Q: What is typology, and why do archaeologists use it?**
A: Typology is the classification of artifacts into types. Archaeologists use it to simplify enormous variability in material culture into meaningful patterns — particularly patterns that reveal how artifacts change across time and space. Meaning lies not in endless data, but in the patterns within that data.

**Q: What is space-time systematics?**
A: The framework archaeologists use to organize artifact data into meaningful spatial and temporal categories. It is the foundation for all higher-level interpretation of the past.

**Q: What is archaeology's greatest strength, according to the chapter?**
A: Its access to vast amounts of time and space. Archaeology can address the entire span of human history — from 3.3-million-year-old stone tools to World War II shipwrecks — across regions too large for any ethnologist to study. What it loses in fine-grained detail, it makes up in temporal and spatial depth.

## Types of Types

**Q: What is an archaeological type?**
A: An abstraction arbitrarily imposed by an archaeologist on a variable set of artifacts. Types are not inherent in the objects themselves — the same artifact can be classified in multiple valid ways depending on the research question.

**Q: What are the three main types of types, and how do they differ?**
A:
- **Morphological type** — purely descriptive; groups artifacts by observable physical attributes (shape, color, size) with no assumed function or chronological significance. Helps communicate what was found without describing every specimen.
- **Temporal type (time-marker)** — a morphological type that has been shown to occur within a specific, limited span of time. Once established, it can be used to date undated contexts where similar artifacts appear.
- **Functional type** — groups artifacts by how they were used, regardless of shape or time. Often crosscuts both morphological and temporal types (e.g., all scrapers used for hide preparation, regardless of material or shape).

**Q: What did Irving Rouse mean by "Classification — for what?"**
A: That typologies must always be built with a specific research question in mind. There is no general all-purpose archaeological classification. The types you create depend entirely on what you are trying to learn.

## What Makes a Good Typology?

**Q: What are the two essential criteria for a good typology?**
A:
1. **Minimize differences within each type and maximize differences between types.** Types must be distinct — overlap or ambiguity destroys the ability to see meaningful patterns.
2. **Be objective and explicit.** The criteria must be defined precisely enough that any trained observer can replicate the results. If it can't be replicated, it isn't science.

**Q: What is an attribute?**
A: A measurable or observable quality of an artifact used to define and compare types. Examples include weight, length, notch position, and proximal shoulder angle. There are no rules on how many attributes to record — use the fewest necessary to accomplish the purpose.

## The Gatecliff Projectile Point Typology

**Q: What was the research goal of the Gatecliff projectile point typology?**
A: To create temporal types — time-markers — that could estimate the age of surface assemblages in the Great Basin where radiocarbon dates cannot be obtained.

**Q: Why was raw material not used as a typological criterion at Gatecliff?**
A: Because raw material (chert, quartzite, obsidian, rhyolite) did not change over time in a systematic way — most artifacts were made of locally available stone. It could not produce useful temporal types.

**Q: What two attributes were primarily used to classify Gatecliff projectile points?**
A: **Weight** (as a measure of overall size) and **notch position/proximal shoulder angle (PSA)** (to distinguish stem shapes). Weight alone defined three size categories (small, medium, large); PSA distinguished contracting from expanding stems among the large points.

**Q: What is the proximal shoulder angle (PSA), and what does it measure?**
A: The angle between the bottom edge of a point's notch and the point's long axis. A PSA ≥ 90° indicates an expanding or corner-notched stem (Elko type); a PSA < 90° indicates a contracting stem (Gatecliff type). It turns a subjective judgment ("expanding" vs. "contracting") into an objective, measurable attribute.

**Q: List the five Great Basin projectile point types from the Gatecliff typology, with their key attributes and date ranges.**
A:

| Type | Weight | Notching | Date Range |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cottonwood Triangular | < 1.0 g | None | Post-700 bp |
| Desert Side-notched | < 1.0 g | Side-notched | Post-700 bp |
| Rosegate series | 1.0–2.5 g | Notched | 1,500–700 bp |
| Elko Corner-notched | > 2.5 g | PSA 90°–150° | 3,500–1,500 bp |
| Gatecliff Contracting Stem | > 2.5 g | PSA < 90° | 4,500–1,500 bp |

**Q: What are "battleship curves," and what do they indicate?**
A: The gradual rise and fall in frequency of an artifact type when plotted across stratigraphic units — the same pattern seen in seriation diagrams. They indicate that a morphological type is also a good temporal type, with a clear period of dominance followed by decline.

## The Frison Effect

**Q: What is the Frison Effect?**
A: The observation by George Frison (University of Wyoming) that resharpening a stone tool can significantly change its shape and size, not just its edge. This means some variation in tool form reflects how heavily tools were used and resharpened, not necessarily different cultural "mental templates."

**Q: How did the Frison Effect challenge Bordes's Mousterian typology?**
A: François Bordes classified Neanderthal stone tools (Mousterian assemblages, 130,000–35,000 bp) into 63 types and four major assemblage groupings, arguing they reflected different cultural groups. Harold Dibble later showed that some of Bordes's scraper "types" (e.g., single-edge scrapers → transverse scrapers) are simply the same tool at different stages of resharpening. This doesn't make Bordes wrong about the existence of patterning, but it complicates the cultural interpretation of that patterning.

## After the Excavation: Lab Work

**Q: What happens to artifacts immediately after fieldwork ends?**
A: First, conservation — stabilizing and preserving the materials. Simple washing is common but can destroy residues (pollen, blood, plant material) useful for reconstructing tool use and diet. Organic or metal artifacts from wet deposits require more intensive treatment (e.g., Carbowax soaking for wood, electrolysis for metal).

**Q: What is cataloging, and why is it essential?**
A: Cataloging records every artifact's provenience (exact find location) and assigns it a unique number, entered into a database. Without precise provenience, an artifact's value to future research is greatly diminished. As a rule of thumb, for every week spent excavating, archaeologists spend three to five weeks cleaning, conserving, and cataloging.

## Space-Time Systematics

**Q: What is a culture area, and what did it lead to in archaeology?**
A: A large geographic region defined (in the 19th century) primarily by what Native American peoples ate. Archaeologists working within culture areas later recognized "subculture areas" — regions with distinctive material culture — which became the basis for **archaeological cultures** (e.g., Hohokam, Mogollon, Anasazi in the American Southwest).

**Q: What is a period in archaeological terms?**
A: A broad slice of prehistoric time defined by gross, easily observable changes in the archaeological record, such as subsistence patterns or house forms. Examples from the Southwest include the Paleo-Indian Period (13,000–7,500 bp), the Archaic Period (7,500–1,900 bp), and the Pueblo Period.

**Q: What is a phase, and why is it archaeology's basic unit of space-time systematics?**
A: A phase is a block of time *and* space defined by one or more distinctive temporal types within a specific region. It is the basic unit because it combines both dimensions — temporal change and spatial variation — into a single, manageable analytical unit. As Willey and Phillips defined it: "an archaeological unit possessing traits sufficiently characteristic to distinguish it from all other units similarly conceived."

**Q: What is the difference between an assemblage and a component?**
A:
- **Assemblage** — all artifacts recovered from a specific unit of provenience (a stratum, a room, or a site).
- **Component** — a culturally homogeneous grouping of assemblages within a single site; assemblages left by people sharing the same culture, even if deposited across different years.

**Q: How are components used to build phases?**
A: Components from multiple sites in the same region are compared. Where they share the same temporal types across overlapping date ranges, they are grouped into a phase. No single site needs to contain the entire cultural sequence — sites complement each other to build the regional picture.

**Q: What is the Yankee Blade phase?**
A: The most recent phase in the central Great Basin (~700–150 bp), defined by ceramics and two projectile point types: Desert Side-notched and Cottonwood Triangular. It is named after a 19th-century silver mine near Austin, Nevada.

**Q: Why are phases kept deliberately vague?**
A: Because they impose discrete boundaries on what is actually a continuous variable (time). Keeping phase definitions flexible allows new findings to refine or subdivide them. As research advances, a phase may be split into subphases — this is a sign of progress, not failure.

## Big-Picture Questions

**Q: What is the logical sequence from individual artifact to phase?**
A: Individual artifacts → grouped by attributes into **morphological types** → tested against independent data (stratigraphy, radiocarbon dates) → elevated to **temporal types** → temporal types cluster within sites into **components** → components from neighboring sites are compared to define regional **phases**.

**Q: Why isn't building space-time systematics the final goal of archaeology?**
A: Because the goals of archaeology are to *reconstruct* and *explain* the past. Space-time systematics is the necessary first step — you must know the *when* and the *where* before you can ask the *how*, the *who*, the *what*, and especially the *why*. Time-markers tell you that something changed; they don't explain why it changed.

**Q: What broader questions does Shoshone pottery illustrate about the limits of time-markers?**
A: Shoshone ceramics appear across the Desert West about 700 bp and end around 150 bp — useful temporal and spatial limits. But as a time-marker alone, the pottery can't answer whether it arrived through migration, diffusion, independent invention, or trade; what happened to pre-700 bp populations; or why pottery appeared when it did. Those questions require analysis of the broader cultural system — settlement patterns, demography, ecology, social organization.

# Chapter 14 Study Guide
## Historical Archaeology: Insights on American History
### ANTH 103 · Kelly & Thomas, *Archaeology* 7th ed.

## Big Picture: What This Chapter Is About

Chapter 14 turns from prehistoric case studies to **historical archaeology** — the study of human behavior through material remains whose interpretation is affected, in some way, by written history. Historical and documentary records are treated as **equally valid but independent lines of evidence**; where they disagree, that disagreement is itself informative rather than "noise" to discard. The chapter traces how historical archaeology evolved from a "handmaiden to history" (supplementing what documents already told us) into an independent field with its own postprocessual flavor, then illustrates three major research themes through case studies: uncovering the **hidden history of African-Americans** (Monticello, the New York African Burial Ground), **correcting inaccuracies** in the public record (the Battle of the Little Bighorn), and **re-examining America's history** to expose power inequalities (the Colorado Coalfield War and the Ludlow Massacre).

## Part 1: What Is Historical Archaeology, and Why Do It?

**Q: How does Kathleen Deagan define historical archaeology?**
A: **"The study of human behavior through material remains, for which written history in some way affects its interpretation."** Some scholars add that because the field covers roughly the last 500 years, it must also grapple with the history of colonialism and capitalism.

**Q: If we already have written records, why bother excavating historic sites at all?**
A: Documentary sources can be superior to archaeology in some ways, but they are also **selective and biased**. Slave owners rarely wrote about slaves' daily lives, and most enslaved people were illiterate and forbidden from writing; authors of documents were generally biased toward their own cultural, political, or ethnic group. Archaeologists treat archaeological and documentary evidence as **equally valid, independent lines of evidence** — differences between the two are as informative as either source alone.

**Q: Who conducted the earliest American historical archaeology, and what made their work notable?**
A: **James Hall** excavated the Standish homestead in Duxbury, Massachusetts in 1853, using gridding, datum points, in-situ artifact plotting, and careful cataloging — methods that (when rediscovered by James Deetz in the 1960s) held up remarkably well against modern standards. **Father Félix Martin** conducted a similar early excavation in 1855 at Sainte-Marie, a former Jesuit mission near Midland, Ontario. Both men were personally connected to the history they excavated (Hall to his Pilgrim ancestor Myles Standish; Martin to his martyred Jesuit predecessors).

**Q: What does it mean to call historical archaeology a "handmaiden to history"?**
A: A phrase from Ivor Noël Hume describing the field's early role: the historical archaeologist as **"a historian with a pen in one hand and a trowel in the other,"** merely supplementing an already-known documentary narrative. This mindset drove projects like Colonial Williamsburg and Plimoth Plantation, which focused narrowly on architectural reconstruction of famous sites (houses of the rich, forts, military sites) rather than broader social questions.

**Q: What changed in the 1960s to push historical archaeology beyond this "handmaiden" role?**
A: **Cultural resource management (CRM)** — federal legislation requiring archaeology in advance of construction — created a boom in both historical and prehistoric archaeology. Historical archaeology became its own specialized field with dedicated journals and professional societies, studying everything from colonial settlements to WWII battlefields.

## Part 2: Three Characteristics and Three Themes

**Q: What three characteristics distinguish historical from prehistoric archaeology?**
A:
1. **A stronger postprocessual slant** — because historical archaeologists often have texts, ethnohistoric data, and oral traditions, they can study meaning and symbolism (ethnicity, religion, status) more confidently than prehistorians can. Bruce Trigger argued the most successful symbolic studies come from historical, not prehistoric, archaeology.
2. **Much shorter time periods** — documentary sources can pin down exact years of site use, in contrast to the centuries-wide windows typical of prehistoric dating.
3. **Emotional and temporal closeness** — the last 500 years record the collision of European, African, Native American, Hispanic, and Asian peoples in creating the modern world, which makes this history personally and politically charged for many descendant communities today.

**Q: What are the three major research themes in historical archaeology today?**
A:
1. **Historically disenfranchised groups** — uncovering the history of African-Americans, Asian-Americans, Native Americans, and Hispanic Americans, whose histories were ignored or biased in the documentary record.
2. **Correcting inaccuracies** — resolving disputes about the recent past the way forensic detectives resolve a crime scene, when history books answer questions unsatisfactorily.
3. **The nature of European colonialism and capitalism** — examining how the developing capitalist economic system affected indigenous and other peoples, often challenging "melting pot" narratives of American history.

## Part 3: Hidden History — The Archaeology of African-Americans

**Q: Where did plantation archaeology begin, and how did the field change after the 1960s?**
A: Plantation archaeology began in **1931 at Mount Vernon**, where landscape architect Morley Jeffers Williams excavated to reconstruct George Washington's garden and outbuildings — with little attention to slavery itself. Prior to the 1980s, most restorations emphasized the "big house" in a nostalgic, "Gone with the Wind" framing, often calling enslaved people "servants." **Charles Fairbanks** began serious **slave archaeology** in the late 1960s, exploring coastal Georgia and Florida plantations and deliberately working to dispel myths of Black biological/cultural inferiority used to justify segregation.

**Q: What was Mulberry Row, and what does its archaeology reveal about Jefferson's slaves?**
A: **Mulberry Row** was the line of 19 buildings — houses and workshops of enslaved people, laborers, and artisans — lining the approach to Jefferson's Monticello mansion. Jefferson enslaved about 200 people. Excavations directed by William Kelso and later Fraser Neiman recovered ceramic hand-me-downs from Jefferson's own table (dated 1770–1800) in the slave dwellings, along with butchered pig, cow, and deer bone — evidence connecting Mulberry Row residents (likely house servants and artisans) to a somewhat better standard of living than field hands housed farther down the mountain.

**Q: What are "subfloor pits," and what do they suggest about how slave housing changed over time?**
A: **Subfloor pits** are rectangular cellars (roughly 3½ × 4 feet, 1–3 feet deep) dug beneath slave dwellings, likely used for storing belongings securely when housemates could not be chosen. Early Monticello houses (1770s) had up to four such pits, suggesting **multiple families shared each dwelling**. Beginning around 1790, houses became smaller, held only a single pit (or none), and had solid interior walls with separate doors — evidence of a shift toward **single-family dwellings** with more privacy. No subfloor pits appear in houses dated after AD 1800.

**Q: How was the African Burial Ground in New York City discovered, and why was it significant?**
A: In **1991**, the bones of some 427 enslaved Africans were discovered beneath a parking lot in downtown Manhattan during construction of a GSA office tower — a site now called the **African Burial Ground**. The Dutch West India Company first brought enslaved Africans to New Amsterdam in 1626; by the eve of the Revolution, New York had the largest enslaved population of any English colonial city except Charleston. Because 18th-century law barred burying Africans in Manhattan's churchyards, the community established its own cemetery, used from roughly 1712–1790 for an estimated 10,000–20,000 burials. The find upended a widespread misconception that slavery was primarily a Southern institution.

**Q: How did the excavation of the African Burial Ground become contentious, and how was that resolved?**
A: The GSA initially prioritized its construction schedule, pressuring archaeologists to excavate skeletons on a rushed timetable; a backhoe accidentally destroyed several burials, triggering protests and a construction blockade. Harlem community leaders objected to being excluded from decisions about a find central to African-American heritage. A **descendant-community advisory committee** was formed, the office pavilion was cancelled in favor of a permanent memorial, and **Michael L. Blakey** (Howard University) led a five-year bioarchaeological research program that treated the descendant community as an "ethical client" alongside the federal government as the "business client." All remains were reburied in October 2003.

**Q: What did Michael Blakey's bioarchaeological analysis reveal about the lives of the enslaved population?**
A: Analysis of roughly 400 individuals found that **half the population died before age 12**, with another mortality peak between ages 15–20. Enlarged muscle attachments and lesions from torn muscles, plus cranial and spinal fractures from carrying heavy loads, showed people were **worked to extreme physical limits**. Yet low rates of dental hypoplasia indicated relatively healthy childhoods in Africa before enslavement — implying the harshest damage occurred after arrival. Blakey also found English colonists were eight times more likely to live past 55 than enslaved Africans.

## Part 4: Correcting Inaccuracies — The Battle of the Little Bighorn

**Q: Why has the popular image of Custer's "last stand" been considered inaccurate?**
A: Because **no U.S. soldier survived** to describe the battle, the popular imagery — Custer calmly commanding a disciplined circle of men, as in paintings by Otto Becker, Frederic Remington, and Edgar Samuel Paxson — was invented after the fact, often for commercial or political purposes (e.g., brewery magnate Adolphus Busch distributed lithographs of "Custer's Last Fight" to 150,000 saloons). Surviving Lakota, Cheyenne, and Arapaho warriors produced very different images: bloodier, chaotic, and lacking any organized "last stand."

**Q: How did Douglas Scott and Richard Fox use forensic archaeology to reconstruct the battle?**
A: After a 1983 brushfire exposed the battlefield, Scott and Fox systematically metal-detected the site, mapping cartridges, bullets, and human remains. Because guns leave a distinctive **firing-pin "fingerprint"** on each cartridge, they could trace individual combatants' movements from spent cartridge locations, and bullet orientation revealed firing direction.

**Q: What did the cartridge evidence show about Custer's position and the nature of the fighting?**
A: Contrary to earlier assumptions, brass cartridges (once thought unique to Custer's rifle) came from multiple .50-caliber guns used by both soldiers and Indians, so **we don't actually know where Custer fought — only where his body came to rest**. There was almost no evidence of formal skirmish lines (which leave evenly spaced cartridge piles), except a tentative one on Calhoun Hill — supporting Indian accounts of chaotic, close hand-to-hand combat rather than an organized defense. Very few Colt .45 cartridge cases were found, suggesting soldiers had little time to draw or reload revolvers before being overwhelmed.

**Q: How did the site's official designation change over time?**
A: In 1991, Congress renamed the site from **Custer Battlefield** to **Little Bighorn Battlefield National Monument**, and authorized a new memorial ("Peace Through Unity," dedicated 2003) honoring the Cheyenne, Lakota, and Arapaho warriors alongside the earlier 1881 memorial to the Seventh Cavalry.

## Part 5: Re-Examining America's History — The Colorado Coalfield War

**Q: What working conditions led to the 1913–1914 coal miners' strike in southern Colorado?**
A: Miners were paid **by the ton, not by the hour**, so unpaid "dead work" (safety measures like shoring ceilings) often went undone; miners faced roughly a **1-in-11 chance of dying on the job**. Many mining towns, including those of the **Colorado Fuel and Iron Company (CF&I)**, controlled by John D. Rockefeller, were company towns where workers who complained could lose both job and housing. The **United Mine Workers Association (UMWA)** organized workers, but the company resisted; when the CF&I refused to recognize the UMWA, roughly 10,000 workers struck in September 1913 and were evicted from company housing.

**Q: What happened at the Ludlow Massacre?**
A: Displaced strikers set up UMWA-provisioned tent camps, including one near the **Ludlow** rail depot. After months of harassment (spotlights, gunfire, a machine gun overlooking the camp), a confrontation on **April 20, 1913** erupted into a day-long firefight between strikers and the Colorado National Guard/company guards. That evening, guards set fire to the camp; **12 women and children died sheltering in a storage cellar** beneath a burning tent. The strike continued for months afterward but ultimately collapsed in December 1914 when UMWA funds ran out; no one was ever convicted. Public outrage led Rockefeller to introduce the **Rockefeller Plan** in 1915 to improve mining-town conditions.

**Q: Why did archaeologists Randall McGuire and Dean Saitta excavate a site already well documented by eyewitnesses?**
A: Unlike Little Bighorn or the African Burial Ground, Ludlow had photographers, journalists, the Red Cross, and named survivors — abundant documentary evidence. The team's goal went beyond recovering new facts: they wanted to use archaeology to **critique historical and ongoing inequalities of power**, pushing the field toward social action rather than pure reconstruction.

**Q: What did excavation of the Ludlow tent camp reveal about daily strike-camp life?**
A: The camp was **deliberately ordered and well-provisioned** by the UMWA, with carefully built storage cellars suggesting strikers expected a long standoff. Despite immigrant miners from many countries (Greece, Russia, Mexico, Japan, eastern Europe), there was no clear evidence of ethnic enclaves. Zooarchaeological remains showed heavy reliance on canned goods and inexpertly butchered meat (consistent with UMWA provisioning), and campers kept good tableware — read by the archaeologists as a desire to project American middle-class respectability. Very little evidence of weapons or ammunition caches was found, despite National Guard claims of rifle pits around the camp; one bullet-riddled coffeepot stands as physical testimony to the barrage the camp endured.

**Q: How did the pre-strike and post-strike neighborhoods at Berwind mining town differ archaeologically?**
A: **Pre-strike** housing (Area K) was irregular — miner-built "vernacular" houses of varied size on boulder foundations, wood-lined outhouses infrequently cleaned. **Post-strike** housing (Area B) was laid out in a uniform crescent of standardized four-room houses on concrete foundations, wired for electricity, with concrete-lined outhouses cleaned on a regular schedule — evidence the CF&I complied with the **Rockefeller Plan's** requirements (porch lights, weekly outhouse cleaning). Post-strike assemblages also contained fewer alcohol and patent-medicine bottles (partly Prohibition, partly the Rockefeller Plan's own alcohol ban) and material evidence (fences, garden plots) of women's shifting domestic role as company policy discouraged taking in boarders and promoted a idealized nuclear-family, "American" household.

**Q: How did the Ludlow project connect archaeology to present-day activism?**
A: The team conducted oral history interviews with miners' descendants, helped identify ancestors' graves and historic photographs, and built an interpretive kiosk and trail at the site (where the UMWA holds an annual memorial). During the project, striking CF&I steelworkers (by then renamed Rocky Mountain Steel) were demanding an end to forced overtime — the same eight-hour-workday demand made at Ludlow in 1913. Archaeologists spoke to the strikers about the Ludlow dead; the steelworkers won their strike in 2004.

4. What did subfloor pits at Monticello reveal about the transition from multi-family to single-family slave housing, and around what date did that transition occur?

5. Why did the excavation of the New York African Burial Ground become contentious, and how was the concept of an "ethical client" used to resolve that tension?
